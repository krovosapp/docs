> *A designer who picks up the Brand Guide knows what to aim for. This document tells them what to actively avoid.*

---

## Why This Document Exists

The Krovos Brand Guide establishes what the visual identity should feel like: sophisticated, warm, trustworthy. The brilliant mentor you never had. Not corporate. Not clinical. Not a dashboard.

But Krovos exists in a crowded visual neighborhood. Financial technology has established strong default aesthetics — dark mode trading interfaces, data-dense dashboards, neon accent colors on black, aggressive urgency typography. Life planning tools default to the clinical: white space, medical-blue color palettes, the visual language of insurance companies.

A designer handed only positive direction will inevitably be pulled toward these defaults. They are visually coherent, professionally executed, and completely wrong for Krovos.

> *Krovos should feel like receiving a letter from someone who knows you. Not like logging into a trading platform. Not like opening a financial wellness app. Not like consulting a government website.*

---

## Anti-Direction 1 — Not a Fintech Dashboard

This is the most critical anti-direction. Krovos lives adjacent to financial technology. The temptation to borrow fintech visual conventions is significant and must be actively resisted.

Fintech dashboards are designed for people who want to watch their money move. Krovos is designed for people who want to stop worrying about their money. These are opposite emotional states.

**Avoid:**
- Dark backgrounds with neon accent colors — green for gains, red for losses
- Data-dense grid layouts where every pixel contains a number
- Animated counters, live-updating figures, and real-time price movements
- Charts as the primary visual metaphor — line charts, candlesticks as hero elements
- Portfolio value displayed prominently as the most important number on screen
- Typography that signals urgency — compressed, high-contrast, optimized for scanning at speed

**Use instead:**
- Warmth over urgency — Midnight Slate and Soft Fog as grounds, not black-and-neon
- Prose and narrative over data density — guidance expressed in sentences, not just numbers
- Progress framing, not performance framing — forward movement, not portfolio returns
- Data displayed in context — numbers always accompanied by what they mean

## Anti-Direction 2 — Not a Life Insurance Brochure

The second visual trap is the clinical aesthetic of financial services, insurance, and institutional planning — the visual language that communicates "we are responsible and regulated" by making everything look as sterile as possible.

**Avoid:**
- Medical blue as the primary brand color
- Stock photography of smiling families, couples on beaches, handshakes in boardrooms
- Geometric sans-serif typography with no warmth or personality
- White and light grey as the only backgrounds — the aesthetic of a hospital waiting room
- Language like "solutions," "offerings," "financial wellness," "your journey"

**Use instead:**
- Ember Orange and Lantern Gold as warmth signatures — human and earned, not institutional
- Real language, not financial services euphemisms
- Visual layouts that feel editorial — something you would read, not something you would file
- Photography (when used) that depicts real situations, not aspirational stock imagery

---

## Anti-Direction 3 — Not a Productivity App

The third visual trap is the clean, minimal, task-oriented aesthetic of productivity tools — the language of getting things done, optimizing workflows, and measuring progress through checkmarks.

Productivity aesthetics are about efficiency. Krovos is about peace of mind. These are related but distinct emotional destinations.

**Avoid:**
- Progress bars and completion percentages as primary UI elements
- Gamification elements — streaks, badges, points, leaderboards
- Task lists and to-do items as the dominant interaction model
- Notifications designed to drive return visits through urgency
- The language of optimization, productivity, and performance

**Use instead:**
- Guidance and narrative as the primary interface, not task completion
- Milestone recognition that is specific and substantive, not generic celebration
- Interactions organized around decisions and understanding, not task completion
- Return visits earned through genuine value, not manufactured urgency

---

## Anti-Direction 4 — Not a Generic AI Product

The fourth visual trap is the generic AI aesthetic — the glowing orbs, the digital particle effects, the robotic sans-serif typography, the language of "powered by AI" and "intelligent insights."

This aesthetic signals technology. Krovos signals relationship. These are fundamentally different communication goals.

**Avoid:**
- Glowing orbs, particle effects, or other generic "AI" visual metaphors
- Robotic or futuristic typography
- Language like "AI-powered," "machine learning," "intelligent insights," "smart recommendations"
- Interface patterns that feel like talking to a computer, not a trusted guide
- The color palette of Silicon Valley — electric blue, white, and grey

**Use instead:**
- Warmth and humanity over technological spectacle
- Language that speaks to the person, not the technology
- The lantern as the brand metaphor — steady warmth, not algorithmic precision
- Interface patterns that feel like receiving a letter from someone who knows you

---

## Anti-Direction 5 — Not a Self-Help Brand

The fifth visual trap is the optimistic, aspirational aesthetic of self-help and wellness — the language of transformation, potential, and becoming your best self.

This aesthetic can feel manipulative because it is often disconnected from reality. Krovos builds trust through honesty, not through aspirational imagery.

**Avoid:**
- Aspirational imagery — people climbing mountains, running on beaches, celebrating achievements
- Language of transformation — "unlock your potential," "become the best version of yourself"
- Bright, high-saturation color palettes that signal positivity regardless of context
- Generic motivational typography and quotes
- Stock photos of diverse groups of happy people

**Use instead:**
- Honest, grounded visual language that reflects real life, not aspirational fantasy
- Language of understanding and navigation, not transformation and optimization
- Celebration with substance — recognition of real accomplishments, not generic encouragement

---

## The Positive Direction

Every anti-direction points toward the same positive alternative: **Krovos looks like the brilliant mentor you never had.**

Think about what it would feel like to receive a letter — carefully considered, written specifically for your situation, by someone who has known you for years and genuinely wants to help. That letter has a particular quality: warm without being saccharine, honest without being cold, specific without being overwhelming, and it leaves you knowing what to do next.

That is the visual and experiential target for Krovos.

### Visual References That Work
- A well-designed financial planning document
- The aesthetic of a trusted law firm or private bank
- A warm, considered editorial publication
- Premium correspondence — the letter, the report, the document
- The emotional register of a trusted advisor's office

### The Test

Before any visual decision, ask: does this feel like the brilliant mentor who knows you?

Or does it feel like a trading terminal, an insurance company, a productivity app, a generic AI product, or a self-help brand?

If it feels like any of the above, it is not Krovos.
