**SECTION 1**

**The Measurement Philosophy**

Most software companies measure activity: DAU, MAU, session length, feature adoption, click-through rate. These are legitimate business metrics. They are also fundamentally the wrong metrics for Krovos.

Activity metrics measure whether people used the product. They do not measure whether the product helped them navigate their lives better. A person who opens Krovos every day but leaves more anxious than when they arrived is not a success. A person who opens Krovos twice a year and makes two major decisions with genuine confidence is exactly the success Krovos is trying to create.

| ***Peace of mind is the metric. Everything else is a proxy.*** |
| --- |

The challenge: peace of mind is not directly measurable in a product analytics dashboard. What is measurable are the conditions that create it and the behaviors that indicate it. Krovos's measurement system tracks those conditions and behaviors — not as substitutes for the real outcome, but as the best available signals of whether the real outcome is being achieved.

**SECTION 2**

**The Outcome Hierarchy**

Krovos measures success against the full outcome hierarchy — from understanding through more living. Each level has measurable signals.

| **Outcome Level** | **What It Means** | **Measurable Signal** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Understanding** | User knows their true situation | *Life Understanding Report generated; report completion rate* |
| **Clarity** | User knows their options | *Priority guidance sections read; info icons engaged* |
| **Confidence** | User knows what to do next | *Next step clicked; action items saved; return visits post-report* |
| **Capability** | User can handle what comes | *90-day retention; Life Graph updates over time* |
| **Peace of Mind** | User is carrying less burden | *NPS score; qualitative feedback; burden reduction survey* |
| **More Living** | User spends less time worrying | *Annual renewal rate; referral rate; long-term retention* |

**SECTION 3**

**The Core Metrics**

**Primary Metrics — The Ones That Matter Most**

**Report Completion Rate**

The percentage of users who complete Smart Start and generate a Life Understanding Report. This is the recognition moment — the foundational product experience. A user who does not reach this moment has not yet experienced Krovos. Target: >70% of users who begin Smart Start complete the report.

**Net Promoter Score (NPS)**

The percentage of users who would recommend Krovos to a friend or family member. NPS is the most direct signal of the trust relationship. The goal is not just high NPS — it is the specific form of NPS where users say "I can't imagine navigating life without Krovos." Target: >60 NPS by 12 months.

**90-Day Retention**

The percentage of subscribers still active 90 days after joining. This is the stickiness signal — it answers whether users found enough value to stay past the initial report. Target: >75% 90-day retention.

**Annual Renewal Rate**

The percentage of subscribers who renew after 12 months. The annual renewal rate is the deepest signal of genuine value. Users renew when they believe Krovos will continue to serve them — when the relationship has proven worth maintaining. Target: >80% annual renewal.

**Burden Reduction Index**

A qualitative metric: do users feel less burdened after using Krovos than before? Measured through periodic surveys with questions like "How would you describe your level of financial and life anxiety before and after using Krovos?" This is the closest proxy to the actual outcome.

**Secondary Metrics — Diagnostic Indicators**

These metrics are useful for diagnosing product health but must never be optimized at the expense of the primary metrics.

**Life Graph Depth Score**

A measure of how completely a user's Life Graph is populated. Deeper Life Graphs produce better guidance and higher retention. Tracked per user over time.

**Document Upload Rate**

The percentage of users who upload at least one document. Document uploaders have significantly higher completion rates, deeper Life Graphs, and better NPS. A leading indicator of long-term success.

**Priority Guidance Engagement**

The percentage of users who engage with (read, save, or act on) at least one priority item from their report. This measures whether the guidance is actionable enough to drive behavior.

**Life Graph Update Rate**

The frequency with which users return to update their Life Graph after major life events. A high update rate signals that users trust Krovos to hold their changing situation — and indicates a healthy long-term relationship.

**Time to Recognition**

The elapsed time from account creation to the "that's exactly me" moment. Measured through report completion velocity and early NPS surveys. Shorter time to recognition correlates with higher long-term retention.

**SECTION 4**

**What We Do Not Measure**

Some metrics are deliberately excluded from Krovos's measurement framework — not because they are technically difficult to measure, but because optimizing for them would compromise the product's core purpose.

**Session Length**

Longer sessions do not mean more value. A user who spends 45 minutes in Krovos because they are confused is worse than a user who spends 5 minutes and leaves with a clear next step. Optimizing session length would incentivize friction.

**Daily Active Users (DAU)**

Most of Krovos's value is delivered in low-frequency, high-impact interactions — the annual life review, the response to a major life event, the generation of a new report. DAU optimization would incentivize manufacturing artificial reasons to open the app.

**Feature Adoption Rate**

Features are not the product. Understanding is. A feature that is rarely used but closes a significant open loop is more valuable than a feature with 80% adoption that generates activity without resolving burden.

**Click-Through Rate**

Krovos is not an engagement-maximization product. CTR optimization would incentivize sensationalism, false urgency, and manufactured anxiety — all of which violate the Laws.

**SECTION 5**

**The Measurement Governance Principle**

Metrics are dangerous when they become goals rather than signals. Goodhart's Law states: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

Every metric in Krovos's framework should be treated as a signal, not a target to be gamed. If a metric is moving in the wrong direction, the correct response is to ask what is happening to the underlying reality it measures — not to find ways to make the metric look better without addressing the underlying issue.

The ultimate test for any metric decision: if a user discovered how we were measuring success, would they feel that our definition of success aligned with their interest? If not, the metric is wrong.
