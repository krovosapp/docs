# Disability Insurance and Personal Illness

## What This Guide Covers

This guide covers the financial picture that emerges when a serious illness or injury removes income, either temporarily or permanently. It covers disability insurance -- what it actually pays, what it does not pay, and what the gaps look like on a real budget -- alongside the decisions that arise when someone is facing a health event that will affect their ability to work.

This guide does not cover general health insurance selection or routine medical expenses. It covers the income replacement side of a serious health situation.

---

## Why This Is Harder Than It Looks

Most people who have short-term or long-term disability coverage through their employer have never read their policy. They know it exists. They do not know what it pays, when it starts, or how long it lasts.

The gap between what people assume disability insurance covers and what it actually covers is often $20,000 to $50,000 per year of lost income. That gap does not become visible until the claim.

Additionally, employer-provided disability policies typically cover 60 percent of base salary. They do not cover bonuses, commissions, RSUs, overtime, or income from a side business. For people whose total compensation includes those components, the real replacement rate is much lower than 60 percent.

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term Disability

### Short-Term Disability

Short-term disability (STD) typically covers the period from your first day of inability to work through 90 to 180 days, depending on the policy. Most employer STD policies have an elimination period -- a waiting period before benefits begin -- of 7 to 14 days.

What this means in practice: if you are injured or become seriously ill, you may have no income for the first week or two. Your emergency fund covers that window.

STD typically replaces 60 to 70 percent of your base salary. It does not extend to long-term disability in all policies -- the two policies are often separate, sometimes from different carriers, and the handoff between them requires documentation.

### Long-Term Disability

Long-term disability (LTD) typically begins where STD ends -- at 90 or 180 days -- and can continue until retirement age in better policies, or for 2, 5, or 10 years in more limited policies.

The definition of disability in the policy matters significantly. Some policies cover own-occupation disability: you cannot perform the specific job you had. Others cover any-occupation disability: you cannot perform any work for which you are reasonably qualified by education and experience. Own-occupation is more favorable to the claimant and is more common in professional-class policies.

### Common LTD Exclusions

- Pre-existing conditions (typically defined as conditions for which you received treatment in the 12 months before coverage began)
- Mental health and substance abuse claims (often covered for a limited period, typically 24 months)
- Self-inflicted injuries
- Disabilities arising from criminal activity
- War and military conflict

## The Tax Situation

Whether your disability benefit is taxable depends on who paid the premium.

If your employer paid the disability premium as an employer benefit, your disability payments are taxable income. Most employer-provided disability insurance falls into this category.

If you paid the premiums with after-tax dollars -- either through a payroll deduction you paid personally or through an individual policy you purchased -- the disability payments are generally not taxable.

This distinction can change your effective replacement rate by 20 to 30 percent. A 60 percent gross replacement rate on taxable disability income may net you 45 to 48 percent of your pre-disability take-home pay.

## Individual Disability Insurance

Employer-provided disability coverage ends when your employment ends. If you are laid off, change jobs, or start your own business, that coverage disappears.

Individual disability insurance is portable, meaning it stays with you regardless of where you work. It also allows you to own the definition of disability -- you can purchase own-occupation coverage that protects your specific profession.

Individual policies are underwritten at the time of purchase, which means pre-existing conditions may be excluded. The earlier in your career you purchase individual coverage, the fewer exclusions are likely to apply and the lower your premium will be.

### Who Needs Individual Coverage

Individual disability insurance makes sense to evaluate for:

- People in high-income professions whose income would be difficult to replace
- Self-employed people and business owners who have no employer coverage
- People with significant financial obligations (mortgage, supporting a family) who could not absorb a 40 percent income reduction
- People whose employer policy covers 60 percent of base salary but whose bonus or commission makes base salary a fraction of total compensation

## When Illness Arrives Before You Are Ready

If a health event arrives before you have adequate coverage or savings in place, the decisions you face are financial triage, not optimization.

### Immediate Actions

**Week 1:**
- File disability claim immediately. Do not wait to see how you feel. The elimination period counts from the date you file, and late filing can delay or void benefits.
- Contact HR about FMLA if you are a W-2 employee. FMLA provides up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave. It does not provide income, but it protects your position while you determine what the illness will require.
- Do not cancel health insurance. If you are unable to work, health coverage is more important, not less.

**Week 2-4:**
- Identify which bills are non-negotiable (mortgage or rent, utilities, food, medical) and which can be deferred or negotiated.
- Contact lenders proactively. Most mortgage servicers, student loan servicers, and credit card issuers have hardship programs that allow payment deferral without default. These programs require proactive contact -- they do not apply automatically.
- If your income stops, file for unemployment if your situation qualifies. A serious illness that prevents work may qualify depending on state rules; some states have paid family leave programs that also apply.

### Rebuilding After a Health Event

When you return to work, the temptation is to replenish savings and continue where you left off. The more durable approach is to use the return to work as a reset point:

- If you drew down emergency savings, replenish before returning to investment contributions.
- If you took on debt during the illness period, work through it methodically before expanding other spending.
- If the illness revealed a gap in disability coverage, evaluate filling that gap when income returns. The lesson from the event is the data.

## Disability and Retirement Accounts

A disability that prevents work for an extended period raises specific questions about retirement accounts.

### Contributions

You cannot contribute to an employer-sponsored retirement plan (401k, 403b) if you are not actively employed. If your disability leave extends past the point where your employer continues your benefits, your retirement contributions will stop.

### Withdrawals

If you need funds during a disability period, retirement accounts may be accessible without the 10 percent early withdrawal penalty in certain circumstances:

- Total and permanent disability, as defined by the IRS, qualifies for penalty-free early withdrawals from retirement accounts.
- The disability must be documented and the documentation must match IRS criteria.
- The withdrawal is still taxable as ordinary income; the penalty is waived, not the tax.

If you are considering withdrawing from retirement accounts during a disability, evaluate all other options first. Retirement account balances take years to rebuild; that time cannot be recovered.

## Social Security Disability

Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) is the federal program that provides income to people who cannot work due to a qualifying disability. Understanding how it works is important because most people significantly misunderstand it.

### Eligibility Requirements

SSDI requires two things: sufficient work history (typically 40 work credits, with 20 earned in the last 10 years) and a qualifying disability.

The Social Security Administration's definition of disability is strict: you must have a condition that prevents any substantial gainful activity, the condition must be expected to last at least 12 months or result in death, and the condition must be on SSA's list or be of equivalent severity.

Most initial SSDI applications are denied. The appeal process can take 1 to 3 years.

### SSDI and Other Disability Income

If you receive both private long-term disability benefits and SSDI, your private insurer will typically reduce your LTD benefit by the amount of your SSDI payment. This offset provision means the total disability income remains similar -- the sources shift, not the total. Your LTD policy documents will state whether and how this offset applies.

### Medicare Eligibility Through SSDI

After 24 months of receiving SSDI payments, you become eligible for Medicare, regardless of age. This is significant for anyone who loses employer health coverage during a long-term disability.

## Protecting Your Business During Illness

If you own a business, a personal illness creates a second layer of financial risk: your business income also stops or is reduced while you are unable to work.

### Business Overhead Expense Insurance

Business overhead expense (BOE) insurance is designed for self-employed business owners and pays ongoing business expenses -- rent, utilities, employee salaries, equipment leases -- during a period of disability, up to a specified monthly maximum.

BOE does not replace your personal income. It keeps the business operational so that when you recover, the infrastructure is still intact.

### Key Person Insurance

If your business has other owners or employees who depend on you, key person life and disability insurance protects the business against the financial impact of losing a key contributor.

## Questions to Answer About Your Current Situation

1. What disability coverage do you have through your employer? What is the elimination period, benefit percentage, and maximum duration?

2. Is your employer disability coverage own-occupation or any-occupation? For how long?

3. Are your disability premiums paid by your employer (taxable benefit) or by you after-tax (nontaxable benefit)?

4. If you became unable to work tomorrow, how many months could you cover your current expenses from existing savings and any disability benefit you would receive?

5. Do you have any individual disability coverage outside of your employer policy?

6. If you are self-employed or have income beyond your base salary, what percentage of your total compensation would a standard 60 percent employer disability policy actually replace?

---

## What Adequate Coverage Looks Like

There is no universal answer, but a reasonable framework:

- Emergency fund covering 3 to 6 months of expenses handles short-term illness gaps before disability benefits begin.
- Disability insurance (employer plus individual if needed) that replaces 70 to 80 percent of gross income, or enough that after-tax replacement covers essential expenses.
- Own-occupation definition, or at minimum a hybrid policy that transitions to any-occupation after 2 to 5 years.
- A clear understanding of what happens to health coverage if you leave employment during a disability period.

The goal is not maximum coverage at maximum cost. It is knowing where the gaps are and making a deliberate decision about which gaps you can absorb and which you cannot.
