# Gig Work

## Build a financial system for income that refuses to arrive on schedule

Gig work and freelance income can create flexibility, autonomy, and multiple ways to earn. It can also move responsibilities that were once handled by an employer directly onto your calendar and into your bank account. Taxes are no longer quietly withheld. Paid time off is no longer built into a paycheck. Health insurance, retirement contributions, disability coverage, bookkeeping, and slow months all have to be funded from the same stream of client or platform payments.

The goal is not to make variable income behave like a salary. The goal is to build a system that works even when the income remains variable.

This guide focuses on five parts of that system:

1. A variable-income budget built around a baseline and a ceiling
2. A cash reserve that can carry essential expenses through a slow period
3. A repeatable process for federal estimated taxes
4. A plan for replacing benefits that an employer would otherwise provide
5. A business structure that fits the work you are doing now

The figures and federal rules below are for 2026. State and local taxes, insurance markets, licensing rules, and business filing requirements vary. This guide is educational and is not a substitute for advice from a tax professional, attorney, insurance agent, or financial planner who knows your full situation.

**Last federal-source review:** July 20, 2026

## The first shift: separate revenue from spendable income

A $2,000 client payment is not the same as a $2,000 paycheck. It may need to cover:

- Platform fees and payment-processing charges
- Supplies, software, equipment, mileage, subcontractors, and other business costs
- Federal income tax
- Self-employment tax
- State and local tax
- Health coverage and other benefits
- Time between assignments
- Personal spending

Treating the full deposit as spendable creates a predictable problem: the bank balance looks healthy immediately after payment and inadequate when a tax bill, annual expense, or quiet month arrives.

Use separate jobs for the money instead. A simple starting account structure is:

- **Business operating:** All client and platform income enters here. Business expenses leave from here.
- **Tax reserve:** A percentage of net business income moves here whenever you are paid.
- **Personal checking:** You pay yourself a planned amount from the business account.
- **Runway savings:** This holds the reserve for low-income months and interruptions.
- **Benefits or long-term savings:** Optional as a separate account, but useful for retirement contributions, insurance premiums, and planned time off.

The accounts do not have to be at different banks. The separation matters because each dollar needs a clear assignment before it feels available.

Use the [Gig Income Tracker](https://krovos.app/gig-income-tracker) as the source of truth for revenue, platform fees, mileage, and deductible business expenses. Do not rebuild those records in a second budget. The budget should use the tracker's net totals to decide how much can move to taxes, personal pay, runway, and benefits. Entries are saved to the Krovos account so the record remains available across devices and after a future re-subscription.

## Build a variable-income budget without averaging your way into trouble

An average can conceal the exact risk a freelance budget needs to address. If six months of take-home business cash are $3,200, $3,400, $3,100, $7,800, $8,500, and $9,000, the average is $5,833. A personal budget built around $5,833 will fail in three of those six months.

Use three numbers instead: the floor, the baseline, and the ceiling.

### 1. Find the essential-expense floor

The floor is the minimum monthly amount required to keep your life and business functioning. Include:

- Housing and basic utilities
- Groceries and essential household supplies
- Required debt payments
- Health insurance and necessary medical costs
- Transportation needed for work and daily life
- Essential business overhead
- Required insurance
- Minimum dependent-care costs
- A monthly share of unavoidable annual bills

Do not include optional extra debt payments, aggressive savings goals, travel, upgrades, or flexible shopping. The floor is not an ideal lifestyle. It is the amount that prevents disruption.

Convert irregular essentials into monthly amounts. A $1,200 annual insurance bill contributes $100 to the monthly floor. A $600 professional renewal due every two years contributes $25 per month. These are often called true expenses or sinking-fund costs.

### 2. Set the baseline personal pay

The baseline is the monthly transfer you can responsibly pay yourself in an ordinary or weak month after business expenses and tax reserves. It should be at or slightly above the essential floor, not at the average of your best and worst months.

If you have at least 12 months of records, look at the lower-income months after business expenses. Exclude a month only if it was genuinely nonrecurring, such as a planned three-month leave that does not reflect the year ahead. Choose a baseline that the lower, repeatable months can support.

If you are new to gig work, build the baseline from committed or highly repeatable revenue, not from a full-capacity projection. Use the **Gig Paycheck Calculator** to test the combination of rates, billable hours, platform mix, and utilization needed to cover the baseline. The point is to see what workload supports the plan before treating that income as dependable.

### 3. Set an income ceiling

The ceiling is the maximum amount you allow routine personal spending to expand to when revenue is strong. Cash above the ceiling is not automatically lifestyle money. It has other jobs: extending runway, funding retirement, replacing equipment, preparing for time off, paying down expensive debt, and supporting defined goals.

A ceiling prevents a strong quarter from creating recurring commitments that a weak quarter cannot sustain. It also makes success visible. Revenue above the ceiling becomes progress instead of quietly disappearing into a larger monthly lifestyle.

### A baseline-and-ceiling example

Suppose your monthly numbers are:

- Essential floor: $3,400
- Baseline personal pay: $3,800
- Personal spending ceiling: $5,200
- Runway target: six months of essential personal and business outflow

The monthly decision works like this:

**When available cash is below $3,800:** Pay yourself from current cash first, use runway only for the remaining baseline gap, pause optional transfers, and reduce flexible spending. Do not raid the tax reserve.

**When available cash is between $3,800 and $5,200:** Pay the baseline, refill any sinking funds used during a weak month, and direct the remainder to the highest current priority.

**When available cash exceeds $5,200:** Keep routine personal spending at or below the ceiling. Split the surplus according to a written rule.

One possible surplus rule is:

- 40 percent to runway until the target is full
- 25 percent to retirement
- 20 percent to business reinvestment or planned time off
- 15 percent to a personal goal or discretionary spending

Those percentages are a policy choice, not a universal formula. The useful feature is that the decision is made before a large payment arrives.

## Build runway for volatility, not just emergencies

An emergency fund protects against unexpected expenses. A freelance runway also protects against expected variability: delayed invoices, platform demand shifts, seasonal work, a client loss, illness, and unpaid time off.

Calculate runway using essential outflow, not gross revenue and not a salary you hope to replace.

Include:

- The personal essential-expense floor
- Essential monthly business overhead
- Health and other required insurance premiums
- Minimum debt payments
- Tax obligations already created by income earned but not yet paid to the IRS or state

Exclude costs that stop when work stops, such as some supplies or per-job subcontractor payments. Keep already-owed tax money in the tax reserve rather than counting it as runway.

Use the **Savings Runway Calculator** to translate the reserve into months at your actual essential burn rate and to model what happens if income falls rather than disappears. A $24,000 reserve means something very different at a $4,000 monthly burn rate than at a $6,000 burn rate.

Choose the target based on the shape of the work:

- A shorter runway can be workable when demand is steady, clients are diversified, household income has another stable source, and fixed obligations are low.
- A longer runway deserves priority when income is seasonal, one client supplies a large share of revenue, payment terms are long, health needs are expensive, dependents rely on the income, or the work could be interrupted by injury.

Build the first month of runway before optimizing investments. Then work toward the full target through surplus-month rules. Recalculate after a major change in rent, insurance, debt, dependents, business overhead, or household income.

## Create a cash-flow rhythm

Variable income becomes easier to manage when the process runs on a schedule even if payments do not.

### Every time money arrives

1. Record the gross payment and associated fee in the Gig Income Tracker.
2. Leave enough in business operating for near-term business costs.
3. Transfer the current tax-reserve percentage to the tax account.
4. Pay personal checking only according to the baseline schedule.
5. Apply any surplus rule after taxes, operating needs, and the next baseline transfer are covered.

### Once each week

- Send invoices and follow up on overdue accounts.
- Categorize transactions while they are still recognizable.
- Save receipts and mileage records.
- Review work booked for the next four to eight weeks.

### Once each month

- Reconcile all platforms and accounts.
- Compare actual net income with the floor, baseline, and ceiling.
- Check runway in months, not just dollars.
- Review unpaid invoices and client concentration.
- Update your Marketplace income estimate if the annual projection has materially changed.

### Once each quarter

- Recalculate estimated taxes using year-to-date results and the rest-of-year forecast.
- Make federal and state estimated payments by the applicable deadlines.
- Review insurance, retirement contributions, and business structure triggers.
- Adjust the tax-reserve percentage if actual profit has moved into a different range.

## Quarterly estimated taxes: what you are actually paying

Freelance workers often hear “save 30 percent” as if that is the tax calculation. It is only a rough reserve rule. Your actual obligation can include several different layers:

- **Federal income tax**, based on taxable income, filing status, deductions, and credits
- **Self-employment tax**, which funds Social Security and Medicare
- **Additional Medicare Tax**, when earned income exceeds the applicable threshold
- **Net Investment Income Tax**, in some higher-income situations involving investment income
- **State and local income tax**, where applicable
- **State business, franchise, sales, or gross-receipts taxes**, depending on location and activity

Federal estimated payments are how you pay tax during the year when enough tax is not being withheld elsewhere.

### When federal estimated payments are generally required

For 2026, estimated tax is generally required when both are true:

1. You expect to owe at least $1,000 after subtracting withholding and refundable credits.
2. Your withholding and refundable credits will be less than the smaller of 90 percent of your expected 2026 tax or 100 percent of the tax shown on your 2025 return.

The prior-year percentage becomes 110 percent if 2025 adjusted gross income was more than $150,000, or more than $75,000 if your 2026 filing status is married filing separately. The prior-year safe harbor requires a 2025 return covering a full 12-month year.

If you had no 2025 federal tax liability, were a U.S. citizen or resident for the full year, and your 2025 tax year covered 12 months, an exception may apply. State rules can differ.

### The 2026 federal due dates

| Income period | Payment due |
| --- | --- |
| January 1 through March 31, 2026 | April 15, 2026 |
| April 1 through May 31, 2026 | June 15, 2026 |
| June 1 through August 31, 2026 | September 15, 2026 |
| September 1 through December 31, 2026 | January 15, 2027 |

These periods are not equal three-month quarters. Put the dates on your calendar. If a due date falls on a weekend or legal holiday, the deadline moves to the next business day. Disaster relief can also change a deadline for an affected location.

### How to calculate a working 2026 estimate

Use the 2026 Form 1040-ES worksheet or tax software. A practical calculation sequence is:

#### Step 1: Project annual gross business income

Add income received so far to a realistic forecast for the rest of the year. Include income whether or not a platform sends an information form. Forms 1099-NEC and 1099-K help report income, but the absence of a form does not make business income nontaxable.

#### Step 2: Subtract ordinary and necessary business expenses

This produces projected net profit, generally reported on Schedule C for a sole proprietor or a single-member LLC using default federal tax treatment. Personal withdrawals are not business expenses.

#### Step 3: Estimate self-employment tax

For a basic estimate when all earnings are below the Social Security wage base:

1. Multiply projected net profit by 92.35 percent.
2. Multiply that result by 15.3 percent.

The 15.3 percent combines 12.4 percent Social Security tax and 2.9 percent Medicare tax. For 2026, the Social Security portion applies only up to the $184,500 Social Security wage base across covered wages and self-employment earnings. The 2.9 percent Medicare portion has no wage-base cap. Additional Medicare Tax may apply above its separate statutory thresholds.

You can generally deduct one-half of self-employment tax when calculating adjusted gross income. That deduction reduces income tax, not self-employment tax.

**Example:** Projected net profit is $66,000, with no wages from another job.

- Net earnings subject to the basic calculation: $66,000 × 92.35% = $60,951
- Estimated self-employment tax: $60,951 × 15.3% = about $9,325.50
- Estimated deduction for one-half: about $4,662.75

This is only the self-employment-tax portion. Federal income tax and any state or local obligation are separate.

#### Step 4: Estimate adjusted gross income and taxable income

Start with projected income from all sources. Subtract applicable adjustments, which may include the deductible half of self-employment tax, qualifying self-employed health insurance premiums, eligible HSA contributions, and deductible self-employed retirement-plan contributions.

Then subtract either itemized deductions or the standard deduction. For 2026, the standard deduction is:

- $16,100 for single and married filing separately
- $24,150 for head of household
- $32,200 for married filing jointly and qualifying surviving spouse

Apply the 2026 tax-rate schedule in Form 1040-ES, then account for applicable credits and other taxes. Qualified business income deductions can affect the result and have their own rules. This is a good point to use tax software or professional help if the return includes multiple businesses, employees, substantial equipment purchases, a spouse's income, health-insurance credits, or an S corporation.

#### Step 5: Compare the current-year estimate with the safe harbor

Calculate both:

- 90 percent of expected 2026 total tax
- 100 percent of 2025 total tax, or 110 percent when the higher-income rule applies

Your required annual payment is generally the smaller figure, reduced by expected withholding. Divide that amount according to the payment method that applies.

The safe harbor can limit underpayment penalties, but it does not make the remaining tax disappear. If 2026 income rises sharply, using the prior-year safe harbor may still leave a large balance due with the 2026 return.

#### Step 6: Choose equal installments or the annualized-income method

Equal payments are simpler when profit is fairly consistent. Divide the required annual estimated amount into four installments, after accounting for withholding.

The annualized-income installment method can better match a lumpy year. It calculates required payments based on income actually earned during each IRS period. This can help when a large project arrives late in the year, but the worksheet is more involved and records must support the timing. See Schedule AI of Form 2210 and Publication 505, or work with a tax professional.

### Withholding can solve part of the problem

If you also receive wages, or a spouse receives wages on a joint return, increasing paycheck withholding can cover some or all of the household's freelance tax obligation. Federal withholding is generally treated as paid evenly throughout the year, which can be useful if estimated payments were missed earlier. Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator and coordinate the change with the full household projection.

### Set a tax-reserve percentage, then reconcile it

A reserve percentage is a cash-management tool. It should reflect:

- Expected federal marginal income-tax rate
- Self-employment tax
- State and local tax
- Deductions and credits
- Wages or other income already using part of the Social Security wage base
- Household withholding

Transfer the reserve each time revenue arrives, ideally after accounting for directly related business expenses. Recalculate quarterly. Do not lower the reserve merely because the account balance looks large. That balance is assigned money.

Pay federal estimates through an IRS online account, IRS Direct Pay, EFTPS, or another approved method, and retain confirmation. Use the correct tax year and payment type. Review state and local payment portals separately.

## Health insurance: shop on total risk, not premium alone

If you do not have access to coverage through a spouse, parent, public program, union, or other group, the individual Health Insurance Marketplace is the main starting point. A self-employed worker with no employees can apply through HealthCare.gov or the state Marketplace. Having a business name or LLC does not by itself turn an individual business owner into a small-group plan.

### Estimate the right income

Marketplace assistance is based on expected annual household modified adjusted gross income, not business gross receipts and not the amount transferred to personal checking. For self-employment, begin with expected net business income after deductible business expenses, then include the other income used in the Marketplace household calculation.

Update the Marketplace when income, household size, address, access to other coverage, or other relevant facts change. Variable income does not require a new report after every invoice, but a material change in the annual estimate deserves an update.

For 2026, the temporary expansion of the premium tax credit has ended. Eligibility generally requires household income from 100 percent through 400 percent of the applicable federal poverty line, subject to specific exceptions. Marketplace savings for a coverage year generally use the poverty guidelines in effect at the start of open enrollment. For 2026 coverage, the applicable 2025 guideline in the 48 contiguous states and District of Columbia is $15,650 for one person and $32,150 for a family of four. Alaska and Hawaii use higher figures. Confirm the amount shown in the Marketplace application for your location and household size before using 400 percent as a planning boundary.

There is another important 2026 change: the prior caps on repayment of excess advance premium tax credit no longer apply for tax years beginning in 2026. If the Marketplace advances more credit than the final tax return allows, the full excess may have to be repaid. Income that finishes above 400 percent of the federal poverty line can eliminate the credit and create a significant tax balance.

To reduce that risk:

- Use a conservative, supportable annual income estimate.
- Update the Marketplace promptly after a major contract, sustained increase, business slowdown, marriage, divorce, or household change.
- Consider taking less than the full advance credit if annual income could cross the eligibility ceiling.
- Keep part of the tax reserve available for premium-tax-credit reconciliation until the annual position is clearer.

### Compare plans in the right order

Do not select a plan by premium alone. Compare:

1. **Network:** Are your current doctors, hospitals, therapists, and pharmacies in network?
2. **Formulary:** Are your prescriptions covered, and at what tier?
3. **Expected annual cost:** Add annual premiums to expected deductibles, copays, coinsurance, and out-of-network exposure.
4. **Worst-case annual cost:** Add annual premiums to the plan's in-network out-of-pocket maximum.
5. **Care rules:** Review referrals, prior authorization, and out-of-area coverage.
6. **Tax features:** Confirm whether the plan is HSA eligible if an HSA is part of your strategy.

Cost-sharing reductions are available only through eligible Silver Marketplace plans. If the Marketplace says you qualify, compare the enhanced Silver option before assuming Bronze is cheaper. A lower premium can be expensive when regular care or prescriptions are involved.

For 2026, an HSA-eligible high-deductible health plan has a deductible of at least $1,700 for self-only coverage or $3,400 for family coverage, with HSA-plan out-of-pocket costs no higher than $8,500 self-only or $17,000 family. The 2026 HSA contribution limit is $4,400 for self-only coverage and $8,750 for family coverage. An eligible individual age 55 or older can generally add a $1,000 catch-up contribution.

An HSA can provide a deduction for eligible contributions, tax-deferred growth, and tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses. It does not make a high-deductible plan appropriate when the deductible would strain cash flow or when the plan's network and drug coverage are a poor fit.

Marketplace open enrollment for 2026 coverage on HealthCare.gov ran from November 1, 2025 through January 15, 2026. State Marketplace dates can differ. Outside open enrollment, a qualifying life event may create a special enrollment period. Medicaid and CHIP enrollment is available year-round for eligible households.

Self-employed health insurance premiums may be deductible in calculating federal adjusted gross income when requirements are met. The deduction has limitations, including rules for months when subsidized employer coverage was available and interactions with the premium tax credit. Coordinate the Marketplace application and tax return rather than treating them as separate calculations.

## Retirement: replace the match with a rule of your own

Without automatic payroll deductions or an employer match, retirement saving can become whatever is left at year-end. Variable income rarely leaves a clean, predictable remainder. Build retirement into the surplus rule and choose an account that fits income, desired contribution level, administrative tolerance, and whether the business has employees.

### Traditional or Roth IRA

An IRA is often the simplest first account because it is separate from the business and does not require a business retirement plan. The combined 2026 contribution limit across traditional and Roth IRAs is $7,500, plus a $1,100 catch-up contribution at age 50 or older. Contributions cannot exceed eligible compensation. Deductibility and Roth eligibility depend on income and workplace-plan coverage.

An IRA can be a practical fit when:

- You are starting with modest contributions.
- Income is still uncertain.
- You want minimal administration.
- You have freelance income alongside an employer plan.

### SEP-IRA

A SEP-IRA allows employer contributions and is relatively simple to establish and administer. Contributions are flexible from year to year, which suits variable profit. For 2026, the defined-contribution limit is $72,000, but the actual SEP limit is also constrained by compensation and contribution-rate rules.

The familiar “25 percent of compensation” limit does not translate to simply multiplying a sole proprietor's Schedule C profit by 25 percent. For a self-employed owner, the calculation accounts for the deductible portion of self-employment tax and the contribution itself. A 25 percent plan rate generally becomes a maximum 20 percent contribution rate on adjusted net earnings. Use the worksheet in IRS Publication 560 or tax software.

A SEP deserves extra review before adoption if you have eligible employees. Employer contributions generally must use the same contribution percentage for eligible employees as for the owner, which can make the plan more expensive than expected.

### Solo 401(k)

A Solo 401(k), also called a one-participant 401(k), is generally for a business owner with no employees other than a spouse. The owner contributes in two capacities:

- As employee, elective deferrals up to $24,500 for 2026, limited by eligible compensation and coordinated with deferrals to other 401(k), 403(b), and similar plans
- As employer, an additional contribution based on compensation or adjusted net earnings

The combined 2026 annual-additions limit is generally $72,000 before catch-up contributions and cannot exceed eligible compensation. The general age-50 catch-up is $8,000 in 2026. For ages 60 through 63, the higher catch-up limit is $11,250 when the plan permits it.

A Solo 401(k) can allow a larger contribution than a SEP at the same moderate profit because the employee deferral is available before adding the employer portion. It also involves more setup and administration. A plan may require Form 5500-EZ after assets reach the filing threshold and in the final plan year. Deadlines and plan-document terms matter.

If the business is taxed as an S corporation, retirement contributions are based on W-2 compensation, not shareholder distributions. This is one reason an artificially low salary can create consequences beyond payroll tax.

### A retirement decision order

Use this sequence:

1. Protect the current year with an adequate tax reserve.
2. Build at least an initial runway buffer.
3. Capture any available match from a separate employer plan.
4. Choose an IRA, SEP-IRA, or Solo 401(k) based on the contribution you can sustain and the administration you can support.
5. Automate a baseline contribution and add surplus-month contributions.
6. Recalculate before year-end so account-opening and employee-deferral deadlines are not missed.

Retirement contributions can reduce current taxable income in some cases, but they do not replace the need for accessible runway. Money in a retirement account is not a substitute for next month's rent, insurance premium, or tax payment.

## Disability insurance: protect the income-producing asset

Health insurance helps pay for care. Disability insurance helps replace income when illness or injury prevents work. A freelancer has no employer sick-leave bank or employer-sponsored long-term disability benefit unless one has been arranged independently.

Start by identifying the gap:

- Monthly essential personal expenses
- Essential business costs that would continue during a disability
- Other reliable household income
- Existing short-term savings and runway
- Public benefits that may apply, recognizing that eligibility can be strict and benefits may be limited

Then compare policies on more than the monthly premium:

- **Definition of disability:** “Own occupation” coverage may pay when you cannot perform your specific occupation, while “any occupation” language is more restrictive.
- **Elimination period:** The waiting period before benefits begin. A longer period may reduce premium but requires more runway.
- **Benefit period:** Benefits may last a set number of years or to a stated age.
- **Monthly benefit:** Confirm what share of essential outflow the policy would replace.
- **Residual or partial disability:** This can matter when you can work fewer hours but cannot return to full earnings.
- **Noncancelable and guaranteed-renewable terms:** Read how premiums and coverage can change.
- **Exclusions and limitations:** Review preexisting-condition, mental-health, substance-use, and occupation-specific provisions.
- **Riders:** Inflation protection, future-purchase options, and other riders can add value and cost.

Coordinate the elimination period with runway. If benefits begin after 90 days, the reserve needs to cover at least that period plus claim-processing uncertainty. If the work depends heavily on physical ability, voice, fine motor control, vision, or another specialized function, the policy definition deserves particular attention.

Also review term life insurance when another person depends on your income, and business liability or professional liability coverage when a client loss, allegation, accident, or data incident could create a claim. An LLC is not a replacement for appropriate insurance.

## Business structure basics

Business structure affects legal separation, taxes, paperwork, cost, and how money moves. It does not change whether the work is profitable, and a more complex structure is not automatically more legitimate.

### Sole proprietor

You are generally a sole proprietor when you perform business activity on your own without forming another state-law entity. Business profit or loss is typically reported on Schedule C with the individual return, and net earnings are generally subject to self-employment tax.

**Why it can fit early work:**

- No separate entity formation is required at the federal level.
- Tax filing and administration are comparatively direct.
- It works for testing an offer, taking initial clients, or earning through platforms.

**What to watch:**

- There is no state-law liability shield between the owner and the business.
- Local licenses, assumed-name registrations, sales-tax rules, and permits can still apply.
- Clean bookkeeping and a separate business account remain important even without an entity.

### Single-member LLC

An LLC is formed under state law. It can create legal separation between business obligations and personal assets when formed, maintained, and operated correctly, but the scope of protection depends on state law and the facts. Personal guarantees, personal misconduct, commingling, and failure to observe required formalities can undermine protection.

For federal income tax, a single-member LLC is generally disregarded by default. Its activity still appears on the owner's return, usually Schedule C, and the owner is generally subject to self-employment tax in the same way as a sole proprietor. Forming an LLC alone does not create federal tax savings.

**Why it can fit:**

- Contracts, banking, and branding can operate under the entity.
- State-law liability separation may be valuable for the activity.
- It creates a platform that can later elect a different federal tax classification if appropriate.

**What to watch:**

- Formation fees, annual reports, registered-agent requirements, franchise taxes, and publication rules vary by state.
- Separate accounts, contracts, records, and signatures help preserve the distinction between owner and entity.
- An LLC does not replace professional, general-liability, cyber, auto, or other relevant insurance.

### S corporation tax election

An S corporation is a federal tax status, not a type of freelance job. An eligible corporation or LLC can elect S corporation treatment. The business files a separate tax return, and an owner who performs services is generally an employee who must receive reasonable compensation through payroll before nonwage distributions.

The appeal is that qualifying profit remaining after reasonable compensation and business expenses may be distributed without self-employment tax, although income tax still applies. The tradeoff is real administration and cost:

- Payroll setup, filings, and deposits
- A separate S corporation return
- Bookkeeping and accountable-plan procedures
- State tax or entity fees
- Rules for owner health insurance and retirement contributions
- Reasonable-compensation analysis and documentation

An S corporation is not a switch that makes all profit exempt from payroll tax. The IRS can reclassify distributions as wages when compensation is unreasonably low. Savings should be modeled after reasonable salary, payroll taxes, professional fees, state costs, retirement effects, and health-insurance treatment.

**When to evaluate it:** Consistent net profit is meaningfully above a defensible salary for the work, the savings exceed ongoing compliance costs by a comfortable margin, and the business can support regular payroll and clean records.

**When to wait:** Profit is low or erratic, the work is still experimental, nearly all profit would be reasonable compensation, or administration would strain cash flow and attention.

There is no universal profit number at which an S corporation becomes correct. Industry, role, hours, location, other workers, capital, state law, and benefit plans all affect the analysis. Ask a tax professional to compare the projected full-year outcome under the current structure and an S election before filing Form 2553.

### A simple structure decision

Use a sole proprietorship when the activity is new, low-risk, and administrative simplicity is the priority. Consider an LLC when state-law separation, contracts, or business identity justify the cost. Evaluate S corporation taxation only after profit is consistent enough to model reasonable salary and compliance costs.

Revisit the structure when:

- Net profit rises and stabilizes
- You hire employees or subcontractors
- Liability exposure changes
- A client requires a particular entity or insurance policy
- You enter another state
- You add an owner
- You seek financing
- Your state changes fees or tax rules

Before forming or electing anything, verify the rules with the secretary of state, state tax agency, local licensing office, insurance professional, and a tax adviser.

## Risks that deserve their own line in the plan

### Client and platform concentration

Track the share of revenue supplied by each client and platform. A business with three income sources can still be concentrated if one provides 75 percent of revenue. Use the Gig Paycheck Calculator to test the impact of losing the largest source and the amount of replacement work required.

### Payment timing

Revenue is not available merely because an invoice was sent. Base transfers on collected cash unless your accounting and tax method requires different treatment. Set deposits, milestones, late-fee terms, and follow-up dates in writing.

### Unpaid time off

Price time off into the year. If you plan to work 46 weeks, do not calculate the required rate as if 52 weeks are billable. Include administrative, marketing, training, sick, and vacation time when using the Gig Paycheck Calculator to set rates or volume targets.

### Business use of a vehicle

Keep contemporaneous mileage and expense records. Platform summaries may not capture all deductible mileage, and not every mile driven is a business mile. Choose the standard-mileage or actual-expense method under the applicable rules and understand how the first-year choice can affect later years.

### Sales tax and local obligations

Federal estimated tax is only one system. Services, digital products, physical goods, and platform transactions can have different sales-tax treatment across jurisdictions. Cities and states may impose business licenses, gross-receipts taxes, franchise fees, or occupational taxes even when federal income is low.

### Worker classification

Calling someone a contractor in an agreement does not control legal classification. If you begin hiring help, review federal and state worker-classification rules before paying that person as an independent contractor.

## State and local routing

Federal rules are only one layer of a gig-work system. Krovos should never infer that a federal calculation resolves state or local obligations.

Use the state stored in your Life Graph to complete this routing sequence:

1. Find the state department of revenue through the [IRS directory of state government websites](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/state-government-websites). Confirm income, estimated-payment, sales-tax, franchise, and gross-receipts obligations that may apply.
2. Find the secretary of state or business-filing office through [USA.gov's state and local government directory](https://www.usa.gov/state-local-governments). Confirm registration, assumed-name, annual-report, and entity requirements.
3. Check county and city licensing where the work is performed, not only where the platform or client is located.
4. Use [SBA local assistance](https://www.sba.gov/local-assistance) when a free Small Business Development Center or Women's Business Center can help identify local registrations and operating requirements.
5. Review the state's worker-classification authority before hiring or paying another person as a contractor.

Save the agency, obligation, due date, and confirmation source in the Guide plan. Do not save passwords, account recovery information, or full tax documents in an ordinary note field.

## When Krovos should prepare a professional handoff

Krovos remains in organize, model, and refer territory. Prepare a concise handoff when any of these conditions apply:

- Income comes from multiple states or countries.
- A worker may be an employee rather than a contractor.
- The business has or plans to add an owner or employee.
- An S corporation election is being considered.
- Marketplace premium tax credits must be reconciled with volatile income.
- Sales tax, gross-receipts tax, or regulated licensing may apply.
- The user is facing an audit, notice, claim denial, contract dispute, or missed filing.
- Business and personal funds have been materially commingled.

The handoff packet should contain the decision being made, known facts, open questions, relevant dates, modeled scenarios, and source documents. The professional should not have to reconstruct the user's situation from scratch.

## A quarterly review that keeps the system current

At the end of each IRS estimated-tax period, answer these questions:

### Income

- What was gross revenue by client and platform?
- What was net profit after business expenses?
- Is the baseline still supported by lower-income months?
- Has one income source become too large a share?

### Cash

- How many months of runway are available at the current essential burn rate?
- Did personal spending stay below the ceiling?
- Were surplus rules followed?
- Are annual expenses fully funded?

### Tax

- Does the year-to-date projection still support the current reserve percentage?
- Is the safe-harbor target or current-year target smaller?
- Would annualized installments better reflect the timing of income?
- Have state, local, sales, and entity taxes been included?

### Benefits

- Does the Marketplace income estimate still match the full-year projection?
- Could income cross 400 percent of the applicable federal poverty line?
- Are retirement contributions on pace and within the correct limits?
- Would the current runway cover the disability-policy elimination period?

### Structure

- Has profit stabilized enough to justify an entity or S corporation analysis?
- Have liability, hiring, ownership, or multi-state facts changed?
- Are accounts, contracts, insurance, and registrations still aligned with the structure?

## The system you are building

Freelance stability does not come from predicting every future payment. It comes from deciding in advance what happens when income is low, ordinary, or high.

The baseline keeps recurring life costs tied to dependable cash. The ceiling prevents strong months from permanently raising the cost of living. The runway absorbs timing gaps. The tax reserve keeps public obligations separate from spendable money. Insurance and retirement accounts replace benefits that no employer is funding. The business structure grows only when the work and risk justify it.

That system will not remove volatility. It will keep volatility from making every decision for you.
