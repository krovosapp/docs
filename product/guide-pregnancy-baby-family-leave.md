## What This Guide Is

Having a baby changes your financial life more than almost any other event. The costs are real, the timing matters, and the decisions you make in the months before and immediately after the birth can have lasting financial consequences.

This guide covers everything from the moment you find out you're pregnant through the end of your parental leave, with special attention to open enrollment timing, the insurance decision almost everyone gets wrong, and the cash flow reality of the first year of parenthood.

## Part 1: The First 30 Days After Finding Out

### The open enrollment timing question

If you find out you're pregnant in October, November, or December, you are holding a time-sensitive financial decision in your hands.

Open enrollment typically runs November 1-30 for January 1 coverage. A baby born in the following year means you will have **predictably large medical expenses** in that coverage year. This is one of the few times in life where you know in advance that your healthcare costs will be high.

**The HDHP vs. PPO decision for a birth year:**

High-Deductible Health Plans (HDHPs) pair with HSAs and have lower premiums. They work well when you are healthy and have low healthcare utilization.

A birth year is not a low-utilization year. You will likely hit your deductible and out-of-pocket maximum.

| | Typical HDHP | Typical PPO |
|---|---|---|
| Annual deductible | $1,500-3,000 (individual) | $500-1,500 |
| Family deductible | $3,000-6,000 | $1,000-3,000 |
| Out-of-pocket maximum | $6,000-8,000 (family) | $3,000-5,000 (family) |
| Monthly premium | Lower by $150-400 | Higher |

In a birth year where you hit the out-of-pocket maximum on either plan, the PPO's lower OOP max (often $3,000-5,000 vs. $6,000-8,000 on HDHP) combined with lower deductible almost always makes the PPO cheaper in total, even after accounting for the higher premiums.

**The calculation to run:**

```
Annual PPO premium cost (your share)
plus PPO out-of-pocket maximum
equals Worst-case PPO scenario

Annual HDHP premium cost (your share)
plus HDHP out-of-pocket maximum
equals Worst-case HDHP scenario
```

In a birth year, you will likely hit the out-of-pocket maximum on both plans. Whichever plan has the lower total of (annual premium plus OOP max) is the better choice for a birth year.

For most people with an October-December pregnancy discovery, the PPO wins clearly for the birth year.

### Maximize your HSA before switching

If you are currently on an HDHP with an HSA, you have a window to maximize your HSA contributions before switching to a PPO in January.

The 2026 HSA family contribution limit is $8,300. If you haven't contributed the maximum, make additional contributions in November and December before your plan switches.

**Why this matters:** HSA funds roll over indefinitely and can be invested. They are triple tax-advantaged: contributions are pre-tax, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free. Money in your HSA today can be invested and used for medical expenses in retirement (where healthcare costs are highest) or withdrawn penalty-free after age 65 for any purpose.

The strategy: maximize HSA before switching to PPO. Once on PPO, you lose HSA eligibility but keep all existing HSA funds.

### Add your baby to your health insurance, qualifying life event

A birth is a qualifying life event. You have 30 days from the birth to add your baby to your health insurance plan. Do not miss this window.

If you miss the 30-day window, you will have to wait until the next open enrollment period. During that time, your baby has no coverage.

Set a calendar reminder for the day your baby is born: "Add baby to health insurance, 30-day window starts today."

### If you're adopting rather than giving birth

Adoption carries a different cost structure and a different set of financial tools than a biological birth. Domestic private adoption typically costs $20,000 to $45,000 depending on the path; foster-to-adopt is often free or low-cost; international adoption commonly runs $30,000 to $50,000 or more.

**The federal adoption tax credit** helps offset these costs significantly. For 2026, the credit is worth up to $17,280 per child and covers qualified adoption expenses including agency fees, legal fees, and travel costs. The credit phases out at higher household incomes, so confirm your eligibility based on your actual income.

Many employers also offer adoption assistance benefits, sometimes several thousand dollars per adoption, that are separate from and can be combined with the federal credit. Check your employer's specific benefit before assuming the full cost falls on you.

The FMLA, state paid leave, and qualifying life event rules described elsewhere in this guide generally apply the same way to adoptive parents as to biological parents, but confirm this explicitly with your HR department, since some employer policies distinguish between the two in ways that are not always obvious from the plan summary.

## Part 2: Parental Leave Planning

### Understanding what you will actually receive

Parental leave policies vary dramatically. Before your leave begins, answer these questions precisely:

1. **How many weeks of leave does your employer offer?** And is it paid, partially paid, or unpaid?
2. **Does your state have paid family leave?** California, New York, New Jersey, Washington, Massachusetts, Connecticut, Oregon, Colorado, Maryland, Delaware, and Minnesota have state-paid family leave programs, with a few more states in the process of rolling theirs out. This list has been expanding, confirm your specific state's current status rather than relying on an older list, since a state without a program today may have one by the time you need it.
3. **Are you eligible for FMLA?** Federal FMLA provides up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave. You must have worked for your employer for at least 12 months and 1,250 hours in the past year, and your employer must have 50+ employees.
4. **Can you use accrued PTO to extend or supplement your leave?**

### California parental leave, the most generous in the US

California has two separate programs that stack:

**California Pregnancy Disability Leave (PDL):** Up to 4 months of job-protected leave for the birthing parent due to pregnancy-related disability (including recovery from childbirth). Does not need to be taken all at once.

**California Family Rights Act (CFRA):** Up to 12 weeks of job-protected leave to bond with a new child. Available to both parents. Can be taken after PDL ends.

**State Disability Insurance (SDI):** Pays approximately 60-70% of your wages (up to the state cap) during pregnancy disability and bonding leave. In 2026, the maximum weekly benefit is approximately $1,620/week. High earners will receive a lower percentage of their actual salary.

**Paid Family Leave (PFL):** Separate from SDI. Covers bonding leave. Also pays approximately 60-70% of wages.

**Total potential leave for the birthing parent in California:** PDL (up to 4 months) plus CFRA (12 weeks) equals up to approximately 7 months of job-protected leave with partial pay through SDI/PFL.

**For the non-birthing parent in California:** CFRA provides 12 weeks of job-protected bonding leave. PFL provides partial pay.

### Calculating your income gap during leave

The gap between your normal take-home and your leave pay is real money that must come from somewhere.

**Step 1:** Calculate your normal monthly take-home (after all deductions).

**Step 2:** Calculate what you will actually receive during leave:
- Employer-paid leave: full pay for X weeks
- SDI/PFL: calculate your weekly benefit times weeks of leave
- Any PTO you're using to bridge gaps

**Step 3:** The gap equals normal monthly take-home minus leave income.

**Example (Sam, UCSF professor):**
- Normal monthly take-home: $4,620
- SDI benefit during leave: approximately $4,100/month
- Monthly income gap: $520

Over 16 weeks (4 months) of leave: $520 times 4 equals $2,080 total gap.

This is relatively small, California's SDI is generous. In states with no paid leave, the gap can be the entire salary for the leave period.

**Build your leave reserve:** Aim to have the full estimated income gap saved before leave begins. This is your parental leave emergency fund, separate from your regular emergency fund.

### Non-birthing parent leave

Many people overlook the non-birthing parent's leave. Take it if you have it, especially in the first weeks after birth when sleep deprivation and recovery are most intense.

If your employer offers paid leave for the non-birthing parent, coordinate timing with the birthing parent's leave so you are not both on leave simultaneously (if that would create a double income gap) unless your finances can support it.

### Employer Short-Term Disability: The Primary Income Source in Many States

For birthing parents in states not listed above as having state paid family leave programs, employer-provided short-term disability insurance is often the primary income replacement available during the physical recovery period after childbirth. This is worth understanding separately from the state paid leave conversation, because the mechanism is different.

Short-term disability (STD) insurance, when offered by an employer, typically covers a portion of income, commonly 60 percent of base salary, for the period of physical recovery from childbirth. The standard coverage period is approximately 6 to 8 weeks for a vaginal delivery and 8 weeks for a Caesarean section, subject to the policy's elimination period. This is a medical leave benefit for the birthing parent's recovery, not a bonding benefit. It applies to the physical recovery period specifically.

STD coverage is separate from FMLA, which is job protection only and does not replace income. The two typically run concurrently: FMLA protects your position while STD replaces a portion of your pay during the same period.

Some employer STD policies require enrollment before a specific cutoff date prior to the pregnancy, so confirming your enrollment status and understanding your policy details early matters more than most people realize.

**Check your employer's STD policy directly, not just the general benefits summary.** The specific answers to look for: what is the elimination period, what percentage of base salary does the policy replace, and how long can benefits continue? If your employer does not offer STD coverage, that recovery period is part of the income gap your parental leave reserve needs to cover.

## Part 3: The Real Cost of a Baby in Year One

### What nobody tells you before you have a baby

The commonly cited "cost of raising a child" figures ($250,000+ over 18 years) obscure the timing. The costs in year one are front-loaded and often shock new parents.

**Year one costs (national average, not including childcare):**

| Category | Annual Cost |
|---|---|
| Medical (birth, well-visits, immunizations) | $3,000-8,000 |
| Diapers | $1,000-1,500 |
| Formula (if not breastfeeding) | $1,500-2,500 |
| Clothing | $500-1,000 |
| Gear (crib, car seat, stroller, etc.) | $2,000-4,000 (one-time) |
| Baby food (after 6 months) | $500-800 |
| **Total (not including childcare)** | **$8,500-17,800** |

### Childcare, the number that changes everything

Childcare is the largest ongoing expense most families don't fully anticipate.

**2026 national average for full-time infant care:**
- Home daycare: $1,200-1,800/month
- Daycare center: $1,500-2,500/month
- Nanny (shared): $1,800-2,800/month
- Nanny (private): $3,500-5,500/month
- San Francisco, NYC, DC, Boston: Add 40-80% to all of the above

**SF infant care:** $3,800-4,500/month for a daycare center. This is not an exaggeration. This is a common real cost for SF families in 2026.

At $4,200/month for SF infant care, that is $50,400/year, equivalent to a second rent payment, or more than many people's entire take-home salary.

**This regional gap isn't unique to San Francisco.** The federal government's own childcare price database shows infant center-based care running roughly $23,000 per year in high-cost states, and under $4,900 per year in the lowest-cost states, for the exact same category of care. The specific number that matters is your own county's, not a national average, since state-level figures alone can understate just how different your actual local cost will be.

**The dependent care FSA:** The IRS allows you to set aside up to $5,000/year pre-tax in a dependent care FSA (DCFSA). This reduces your taxable income and saves approximately $1,250-1,750/year in taxes depending on your bracket.

For $50,400/year in childcare costs, the FSA saves a relatively small percentage, but it's still $1,500+ in tax savings you shouldn't leave on the table.

**Elect the DCFSA during open enrollment.** This must be done during the annual enrollment window. You cannot add it mid-year unless you have a qualifying life event (which a birth is, but enrollment windows are narrow).

### The Trump Account (ABLE account for children born after January 1, 2025)

Children born on or after January 1, 2025 may be eligible for the "Trump Account," a federally-funded savings account with an initial $1,000 government contribution.

Enroll as soon as possible after birth. The account is tax-advantaged and grows over time. Contributions from family members are allowed up to federal limits.

## Part 4: Estate Planning With a New Dependent

### The single most important document you don't have

If you have a child and no will, a judge, not you, will decide who raises your child if something happens to both parents.

A guardian designation in your will is the legal instruction that tells the court who you want to raise your child. Without it, the decision is left to the court's discretion.

**Create a will with guardian designation within 30 days of birth.** This is not optional. It is not something to do "eventually." It is a document your child needs you to have.

### What your estate plan should include after a baby

1. **Will** with guardian designation for minor children
2. **Healthcare directive / living will**, your medical wishes if you are incapacitated
3. **Durable power of attorney**, who makes financial decisions if you cannot
4. **Beneficiary designations updated** on all retirement accounts and life insurance
5. **Life insurance**, does each parent have enough coverage if the other dies?

### Life insurance, how much do you need?

With a new dependent, the life insurance question becomes urgent.

A general framework: 10-12x your annual income in term life insurance coverage. This provides enough for the surviving parent to:
- Pay off the mortgage
- Replace lost income for 10+ years
- Fund college
- Maintain their financial position

At $195,000 annual income (Alex), that's approximately $1.95-2.3M in coverage. Term life insurance for a healthy 32-year-old costs approximately $50-80/month for a 20-year $1M policy. Two policies for both partners: $100-160/month total.

This is one of the highest-value financial decisions you will make as a new parent. Delay is risk.

## Part 5: Choosing Childcare and Sharing the Load

### Evaluating childcare beyond price

When touring a childcare option, look past cost to the things that actually predict a good fit: caregiver-to-child ratios compared against your state's minimum (bare-minimum ratios often mean a more chaotic room), how long lead caregivers typically stay versus how often staff turns over, and whether their approach to sleep, feeding, and discipline broadly matches your own values. Watch the emotional climate directly: are babies talked to, held, and soothed responsively, or left waiting. A common regret is choosing based purely on convenience or price and only realizing the fit was wrong after enrolling.

### Baby gear: buy for use, not aesthetics

The most commonly regretted purchases are impulse buys that don't earn their space: multiple "container" products (bouncers, loungers, swings) that overlap in function and mostly go unused, oversized travel systems that become a burden to lift and fold in real life, and excessive newborn clothing that's outgrown before it's worn. Favor gear that adapts as your child grows (a convertible car seat, a crib that becomes a toddler bed) over single-purpose items, and prioritize how something actually functions in your specific space and routine over how it looks.

### Dividing household and childcare labor deliberately

The most common failure pattern after a baby arrives isn't unequal effort, it's invisible, undiscussed division. One partner, often the birthing parent, ends up holding nearly every task by default, including the mental load of remembering and planning, while the other partner stays in a reactive "just tell me what to do" role. This isn't solved by one partner "helping" more; it's solved by explicitly assigning full ownership, noticing the need, planning it, and following through, of specific recurring tasks to each partner, then revisiting that division regularly as life changes. This approach has a name, the Fair Play method, and its core insight is simple: division of labor has to be explicit and renegotiated on purpose, not assumed.

**Practical steps that reduce friction in the first year:** agree on concrete on-duty and off-duty blocks so neither partner is silently always on call, use a shared calendar for appointments and tasks instead of relying on memory, and protect small, regular moments of connection, a short walk, ten minutes of phone-free time, rather than assuming a full date night is realistic. Naming the mental load out loud, not just the physical tasks, is often what actually changes the pattern.

## Action Plan

**When you find out you're pregnant:**
- [ ] Calculate open enrollment timing, should you switch from HDHP to PPO?
- [ ] Maximize HSA contributions before any plan switch
- [ ] Elect dependent care FSA for the birth year during open enrollment
- [ ] Calculate your parental leave income gap
- [ ] Start building your leave reserve

**Before the birth:**
- [ ] Create will with guardian designation
- [ ] Get term life insurance for both parents
- [ ] Update beneficiaries on all accounts
- [ ] Research childcare options and get on waitlists early (SF waitlists can be 6-12 months)
- [ ] Confirm parental leave paperwork with HR, both parents

**At birth:**
- [ ] Set calendar reminder: add baby to health insurance within 30 days
- [ ] Enroll in Trump Account if eligible
- [ ] File for SDI/PFL in California (or your state equivalent) within 10 days of leave start

**In the first year:**
- [ ] Open a 529 account for the baby, start with whatever you can
- [ ] Review budget, the real monthly costs will now be clear
- [ ] Schedule the 6-month financial check-in: is leave reserve sufficient? Is childcare budget accurate?
