# Widowhood and Losing a Partner

## What This Guide Covers

This guide covers the financial decisions that arise after losing a spouse or long-term partner. It is organized by urgency -- what must happen in the first days, what should happen in the first weeks and months, and what can wait until later without penalty.

The financial tasks in this period are real and time-sensitive. None of them require you to be at your best. They require you to know what exists and to get the right people involved at the right time.

---

## The First Days

There are a small number of things that genuinely cannot wait.

### Death Certificates

Order more than you think you will need. A minimum of 10 to 15 certified copies is a reasonable starting point. Banks, insurance companies, retirement account custodians, government agencies, and financial institutions will each require an original. You cannot photocopy a certified death certificate for most purposes. Running short of copies adds delay and administrative friction at a time when that cost is highest.

### Immediate Bills

Automatic payments and recurring bills will continue regardless of what has happened. Housing costs, utilities, and any joint obligations continue on schedule. If you are uncertain what is set to auto-pay from joint accounts, do not cancel cards or close accounts immediately. Give yourself time to identify what is actively running.

### Life Insurance

If there is a life insurance policy, contact the insurer as early as possible. The claims process typically takes several weeks. You will need the death certificate and the policy itself, or at minimum the policy number and insurer name. The sooner the claim is filed, the sooner the benefit is paid.

Life insurance death benefits are generally not taxable. They are paid directly to the named beneficiary and are not subject to probate.

## The Estate and What Happens to It

### Probate

Probate is the legal process through which a court validates a will and supervises the distribution of the estate. Not all assets go through probate.

Assets that typically avoid probate:
- Assets with named beneficiaries (life insurance, retirement accounts, POD bank accounts)
- Joint tenancy property
- Assets in a trust

Assets that typically go through probate:
- Property titled in the deceased person's name alone without a beneficiary designation
- Bank accounts without POD (payable on death) designation

Probate timelines vary significantly by state and by the complexity of the estate. Simple estates in states with simplified small-estate procedures may close in a few months. Complex estates or those involving disputes can take a year or more.

If your partner had a will, find it. If they had a trust, locate the trust documents. An estate attorney can guide the probate process and is worth engaging early.

### When There Is No Will

Dying without a will is called dying intestate. In this case, state law determines how the estate is distributed. For legally married spouses, most states provide significant protections. For unmarried partners, the intestate laws in most states do not recognize the relationship, which means the partner has no legal claim to assets held solely in the deceased person's name.

If you were an unmarried partner and your partner died without a will naming you as a beneficiary, consult an estate attorney immediately. The legal path is more difficult but not necessarily closed.

## Retirement Accounts and Beneficiary Designations

Retirement accounts -- 401k, 403b, IRA, Roth IRA -- are transferred by beneficiary designation, not by will. The beneficiary designation on file with the plan custodian controls where the money goes, regardless of what the will says.

### Spousal Beneficiary

A surviving spouse has options that no other beneficiary has.

**Rollover to own IRA**: The surviving spouse can roll the inherited retirement account into their own IRA, treating it as their own. The RMD schedule then follows their own age. This is generally the most tax-efficient approach for spouses who do not need the money immediately and are themselves under retirement age.

**Keep as inherited IRA**: The surviving spouse can keep the account as an inherited IRA and take distributions based on their own life expectancy. This option gives more flexibility on distribution timing.

**Direct distribution**: The surviving spouse can take the entire balance as a lump-sum distribution. This is generally the least tax-efficient option because the entire amount is taxable in the year received.

### Non-Spouse Beneficiaries

If you were not legally married, the rules are less favorable. Non-spouse beneficiaries must generally withdraw the entire inherited account within 10 years of the original account owner's death (the 10-year rule, under SECURE Act 2.0 provisions effective 2024).

### Required Minimum Distributions in the Year of Death

If the person who died was already taking required minimum distributions (RMDs), and those RMDs had not been fully taken in the year of death, the remaining RMD for that year must be taken by December 31. This obligation transfers to the estate or beneficiary. Missing the year-of-death RMD carries a significant IRS penalty.

## Social Security Survivor Benefits

Social Security survivor benefits are one of the most underused financial resources available to surviving spouses. They are also commonly misunderstood.

### Who Is Eligible

A surviving spouse is generally eligible if the marriage lasted at least 9 months. Divorced spouses may also be eligible if the marriage lasted at least 10 years. Dependent children may be eligible regardless of the marriage duration.

### What the Benefit Pays

A surviving spouse is entitled to up to 100 percent of the deceased spouse's Social Security benefit if the survivor has reached full retirement age, or a reduced amount beginning at age 60 (or age 50 if disabled).

A surviving spouse can receive survivor benefits on the deceased spouse's record while allowing their own Social Security benefit to continue growing until age 70 -- and then switch to their own benefit if it becomes larger. This sequencing can substantially increase lifetime Social Security income.

### When to File

Survivor benefits do not accrue retroactively in most circumstances. If you are eligible, file promptly. Contact the Social Security Administration directly to understand the filing process. The agency will require the death certificate, your own Social Security number and the deceased's, and the marriage certificate.

### If You Are Receiving Your Own Social Security

If you are already receiving your own Social Security benefit, you cannot receive both your own benefit and the full survivor benefit simultaneously. You receive the higher of the two. A Social Security specialist can help you understand the optimal sequencing.

## Pension Survivor Benefits

If your partner had a pension, the survivor benefit provisions of that pension are governed by the pension plan documents and by whether a survivorship option was elected at the time of retirement.

Federal law (ERISA) requires that married pension participants provide spousal consent before waiving the standard survivor annuity. If your partner was receiving a pension and was married to you at the time they elected their benefit, you should be receiving a survivor benefit unless both of you signed a waiver of that benefit.

Contact the pension plan administrator to understand what survivor benefit, if any, is due and to initiate the claim. You will need the death certificate and proof of marriage.

## Joint Accounts and Titling

### Bank Accounts

A joint checking or savings account with right of survivorship passes automatically to the surviving account holder. No probate required. The bank will typically need a death certificate to remove the deceased person from the account.

A bank account held in the deceased person's name alone, without a POD designation, goes through probate.

### Investment Accounts

Brokerage accounts work similarly. Joint accounts with JTWROS (joint tenants with right of survivorship) pass to the survivor. Individual accounts go through the beneficiary designation if one is named, or through probate if not.

Step-up in cost basis: assets held in a taxable brokerage account receive a step-up in cost basis at the owner's death. This means the cost basis of the inherited assets is reset to the value on the date of death, eliminating capital gains tax on appreciation that occurred during the deceased person's lifetime. This is one of the most significant tax provisions for inherited investments.

### Real Estate

Real estate titled as joint tenants with right of survivorship passes to the survivor. Real estate titled as tenants in common goes through probate, and the deceased person's share passes per their will.

## Updating Your Own Documents

After handling the immediate priorities, update your own beneficiary designations, will, and healthcare documents. A common and serious error is allowing outdated documents to go unchanged after a major life event. The person who died may still be named as a beneficiary on your own accounts. Update these designations to reflect current reality.

If the person who died was named as your executor or as your children's guardian in your own estate documents, that designation is no longer valid and should be replaced as soon as you are able. A will that names a deceased person as executor or guardian does not fail quietly: it creates real administrative delay and may require court intervention to resolve, at a time when your family has enough to navigate without it.

Documents to review and update:
- Will
- Healthcare proxy and medical power of attorney
- Life insurance beneficiary designations
- Retirement account beneficiary designations
- Bank account POD designations

## Taxes in the Year of Death and After

### Filing Status

In the year of death, if you were married to the person who died, you can still file as married filing jointly for that year (if you have not remarried). This typically results in a lower tax rate than filing as single.

**Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS) status** is available for the two tax years following the year of death, giving you three total years of favorable treatment (the year of death plus two more). To qualify, all three of the following must be true:

- You did not remarry before the end of the year for which you are claiming the status
- A qualifying child lived with you for the entire year (standard temporary absence exceptions apply, such as school or medical treatment)
- You were eligible to file jointly in the year your spouse died

QSS status carries the same tax rates as married filing jointly, which is significantly more favorable than the single rate. This is one of the most commonly overlooked tax benefits available to recent widows and widowers.

After the two-year QSS window closes, your filing status depends on your household: if a qualifying child still lives with you, Head of Household status typically applies. If no qualifying child is present, your status reverts to Single. Head of Household carries more favorable rates and a higher standard deduction than Single. See IRS Publication 501 for the complete rules governing each filing status.

### The Estate Tax Return

If the estate's gross value exceeds the federal estate tax exemption ($15,000,000 per person for 2026 -- confirmed: IRS.gov estate tax filing threshold table and IRS tax year 2026 inflation adjustments release; figure is indexed annually for inflation, confirm the current-year amount with a qualified estate attorney before filing; see also the Krovos Estate Planning Guide for legislative background on the 2025 law change), a federal estate tax return (Form 706) is required within 9 months of death. State estate and inheritance tax thresholds vary significantly and are often lower than the federal threshold. An estate attorney or CPA who works on estate matters can identify whether any state-level filing is required.

### The Final Income Tax Return

A final individual income tax return (Form 1040) must be filed for the deceased person covering the period from January 1 through the date of death. Income earned or received up to the date of death is reported on this return. If you were married and filing jointly, this return can be filed jointly in the year of death.

## Getting Help

The financial tasks in this period are significant, and there is no requirement to handle them alone.

An estate attorney is appropriate for probate, asset transfer, will validation, and any disputes.

A CPA or tax advisor familiar with estate matters is appropriate for the final income tax return, estate tax filings, and inherited retirement account tax questions.

A fee-only financial advisor can help with the longer-term questions: how to restructure a budget that was built for two, how to evaluate survivor Social Security claiming strategies, and how to integrate an inherited lump sum into a financial plan.

A Social Security Administration representative can walk you through survivor benefit options specific to your situation. This conversation is free and worth having before making any claiming decision.

---

## What Can Wait

Not everything is urgent. These items can wait until the immediate period has stabilized:

- Investment account consolidation and simplification
- Long-term budget restructuring
- Major financial decisions (selling a home, making large investments)
- Revising long-term retirement projections

Give yourself permission to defer non-urgent decisions. The financial system does not require you to rebuild your life in the first 90 days.
