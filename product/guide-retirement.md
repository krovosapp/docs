## What This Guide Is

Retirement planning changes character once retirement is close enough to count in years rather than decades. The questions shift from "am I saving enough" to specific, sometimes irreversible decisions: when to claim Social Security, how to sequence withdrawals across accounts, and how to shift from a lifetime of saving to actually spending what you built. This guide covers those decisions directly.

---

## Part 1: When You Can Actually Afford to Stop

### Your real number, not a borrowed one

"Retirement age" is often treated as a cultural default, whatever age a parent retired at, or whenever Social Security or a pension becomes available, rather than a number calculated from your actual situation. Your real number depends on your projected income from all sources against your actual expected expenses, not a generic percentage rule applied without context.

Calculate your projected retirement income from every source: Social Security, retirement account withdrawals, any pension, and other income. Compare it against your actual expected expenses in retirement, which are often different from your current expenses, sometimes lower due to no more commuting or mortgage payoff, sometimes higher due to healthcare or travel.

## Part 2: Claiming Decisions You Can't Take Back

### Social Security timing

Full retirement age for Social Security is 66 to 67 depending on birth year. Claiming before full retirement age permanently reduces your monthly benefit; claiming after full retirement age, up to age 70, permanently increases it, by roughly 8 percent per year of delay.

This is not automatically "wait as long as possible." The right answer depends on your health, your family longevity, whether you need the income sooner, and whether you are still working, since working while claiming early can temporarily reduce benefits further until full retirement age.

**Model your claiming decision at two or three different ages before deciding**, rather than defaulting to the earliest available date out of habit or the latest out of a generic assumption that later is always better.

### Social Security and taxes

Depending on your total income in retirement, up to 85 percent of your Social Security benefit can be subject to federal income tax. The income thresholds that determine this are not adjusted for inflation, so more retirees are affected by this over time than in past decades. A small number of states also tax Social Security benefits at the state level; most do not.

### The withdrawal rate actually depends on your retirement age

The commonly cited "4 percent rule" is a starting point, not a single correct number. Fidelity's published guidance ties a sustainable withdrawal rate directly to the age you retire: retiring at 62 supports roughly a 3.9 percent withdrawal rate, 67 supports roughly 4.5 percent, and 70 supports roughly 4.9 percent, since retiring later means a shorter payout horizon and more Social Security income covering your expenses. Retiring meaningfully before 62 generally calls for a lower rate, closer to 3 percent, with a correspondingly larger savings target, since Social Security is delayed and the payout horizon is longer.

**If you are retiring before Social Security begins, treat that gap as its own distinct phase, not an extension of your full retirement math.** The years between an early retirement and the start of Social Security are a finite bridge: you can often draw down your portfolio faster during that specific window than the standard rule would suggest, because Social Security will take over part of the income burden once it starts and the withdrawal horizon for those bridge-period dollars is genuinely shorter. Treating your entire retirement as one uniform withdrawal rate can lead to saving significantly more than you actually need, particularly if you plan to retire early.

## Part 3: What Happens to Your Income Sources

Retirement income typically comes from several places at once: Social Security, tax-deferred accounts like traditional 401k and IRA balances, potentially a Roth account, and taxable brokerage accounts. The order you draw from these affects both your tax bill and how long your money lasts.

A commonly used general sequence is spending taxable account funds first, then tax-deferred accounts, then Roth accounts last, since Roth withdrawals are tax-free and benefit the most from continued tax-free growth. This is a starting framework, not a universal rule; your specific tax situation each year may justify deviating from it.

**Required minimum distributions (RMDs)** from traditional retirement accounts currently begin at age 73 for most people. Missing an RMD carries a real penalty, so this deadline needs to be tracked precisely once it applies to you.

## Part 4: The Shift From Saving to Spending

Decades of a "save more" mindset do not automatically translate into comfort spending down a balance you spent a lifetime building. This is a genuine psychological adjustment, not just a math exercise, and it catches many new retirees off guard.

Build an actual monthly spending plan for the first year of retirement, not just a total savings target. Knowing you have $1.2 million saved is not the same as knowing what your actual monthly budget looks like and confirming your income sources cover it.

## Part 5: Retiring Before the Standard Age

### The two gaps early retirement creates

Retiring before 65 creates two specific gaps that traditional retirement planning does not address: a healthcare gap before Medicare eligibility, and a potential penalty gap on retirement account withdrawals before age 59 and a half.

**The healthcare gap:** Between early retirement and Medicare eligibility at 65, you need a coverage plan: COBRA from your last employer for up to 18 months, a marketplace plan, or a spouse's employer coverage if applicable. Marketplace premiums can be substantial without employer subsidies, so this cost needs to be built into your early retirement budget explicitly, not treated as an afterthought.

**The early withdrawal gap:** Withdrawals from traditional 401k and IRA accounts before age 59 and a half typically incur a 10 percent penalty in addition to ordinary income tax. Two specific exceptions matter for early retirees: the Rule of 55, which allows penalty-free 401k withdrawals if you leave your job in or after the year you turn 55, though this applies only to the 401k of the employer you just left, not IRAs. And Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPP, sometimes called 72(t) distributions), which allow penalty-free access to IRA funds before 59 and a half if you commit to a specific, IRS-defined withdrawal schedule for at least 5 years or until age 59 and a half, whichever is longer.

### Bridge accounts matter more for early retirees

If early retirement is the goal, prioritize building savings in taxable brokerage accounts and Roth accounts (where contributions, though not earnings, can be withdrawn penalty-free at any time) specifically to bridge the years before penalty-free access to traditional retirement accounts. A portfolio that is 100 percent in traditional 401k and IRA accounts is poorly structured for anyone planning to retire meaningfully before 59 and a half, regardless of the total balance.

## Part 6: Life After the Paycheck

### Plan for what you're retiring to, not just from

Many new retirees are surprised by how much of their identity was tied to their job, their title, their colleagues, their daily structure. Satisfaction in retirement correlates strongly with having a real sense of contribution and routine, through volunteering, part-time work, mentoring, or community involvement, rather than treating retirement as unstructured time. Before you retire, inventory what you actually enjoyed about work beyond the paycheck, problem-solving, teaching, organizing, and look for ways to keep exercising that outside a job.

### Design your actual weekly rhythm

Map a realistic week that includes social contact, physical activity, and mentally engaging projects, not just leisure. A common pattern: an initial "honeymoon" period of freedom followed by a slump once the novelty fades and the absence of daily structure sets in. Treating this as a normal part of the transition, not a sign something's wrong, makes it easier to move through.

### When partners retire at different times

Discuss expectations directly: how household tasks shift when one partner is home more, how the still-working partner's stress gets acknowledged, and which time is reserved for shared activities versus independent pursuits. Resentment tends to build when a working partner feels the retired one is "on vacation," or when the retired partner feels isolated during the workweek. Naming these dynamics ahead of time, and encouraging the retired partner to build independent structure rather than waiting at home, heads off most of the friction.

## Action Plan

**5 or more years out:**
- [ ] Calculate your real retirement number using actual expected expenses, not a percentage rule of thumb
- [ ] Model your Social Security claiming decision at two or three different ages
- [ ] Confirm your current retirement contributions are on pace for your calculated number

**1 to 2 years out:**
- [ ] Build a projected monthly retirement budget based on actual expected expenses
- [ ] Decide your account withdrawal sequence and how it interacts with your tax situation
- [ ] Finalize your Social Security claiming age decision

**At and after retirement:**
- [ ] Track your required minimum distribution deadlines starting at age 73
- [ ] Revisit your spending plan after the first year against what actually happened
- [ ] Reassess your withdrawal sequence annually as your tax situation changes
