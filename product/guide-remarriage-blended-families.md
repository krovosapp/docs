# Blended Family

## The outcome

This upgrade turns competing obligations into a shared, documented household plan. Krovos reuses the members' existing plans and asks each partner to confirm only what changed. It produces a saved agreement map and professional handoff without another AI report.

## Start separately, then decide together

Each partner privately confirms assets, debts, income, support orders, dependents, businesses, beneficiary designations, education commitments, estate documents, and financial boundaries. Krovos then creates a shared facts view. It does not force equal ownership, equal contributions, or account consolidation.

## Protect existing obligations

Court orders, property agreements, child support, alimony, custody terms, and existing beneficiary commitments are recorded before new household decisions. The plan flags conflicts for separate legal or tax review rather than interpreting them.

## Build the household operating agreement

Partners choose how to handle shared bills, separate obligations, emergency reserves, home ownership and improvements, gifts to children, education support, caregiving, privacy, and decision authority. Each decision stores what is shared, what stays separate, who pays, how it is reviewed, and what happens after job loss, disability, separation, or death.

## Child-specific scenarios

Krovos creates one record per child or dependent: residence and custody facts, support, insurance, education expectations, disability or care needs, existing accounts, parent/contributor questions, and promised inheritance. It does not assume every child should receive the same amount; it makes differences visible and intentional.

The FAFSA Remarriage Planner uses the applicable award year's official contributor rules and deadlines. It does not estimate aid from marriage timing or present marriage as a financial-aid tactic.

## Estate and beneficiary conflict screen

The Blended Family Inheritance tool inventories homes, retirement accounts, insurance, businesses, jointly owned assets, personal property, and beneficiary-controlled accounts. It shows where the current title or designation may conflict with a stated intention. It does not recommend a trust.

The QTIP vs. Bypass Comparison is an attorney-preparation walkthrough, not a legal or tax recommendation. It records goals, state, family structure, control concerns, and questions for independent counsel. Current exemptions and state rules must be verified at the time of planning.

## The professional handoff

Krovos prepares separate question packets for family-law counsel, estate counsel, tax professionals, financial-aid offices, insurers, and benefits administrators. Sensitive partner inputs are not exposed without consent.

## The 90-day plan

- **Now:** disclosures, existing orders, coverage, beneficiaries, and urgent conflicts.
- **30 days:** household agreement, child records, cash-flow choices, and professional appointments.
- **60 days:** ownership and beneficiary confirmation, education and care plans, and document drafting.
- **90 days:** signed documents where appropriate, stored records, responsible owners, and review dates.

## Sources and review standard

Use current court orders, plan and account records, Federal Student Aid guidance, Social Security and IRS sources, and state-specific independent counsel. Review after marriage, birth, graduation, support changes, relocation, disability, separation, or death.
