# Guide Competitive Benchmarks

**Established:** July 17, 2026  
**Last verified:** July 20, 2026
**Status:** Satisfies Criterion 2 (Competitive Benchmark) of the six-criterion rubric in `guide-approval-criteria.md`. One section per guide: What Exists in the Market, What's Missing, Krovos Differentiation Claim. The original findings were synthesized from Perplexity research passes; the three gaps (Early Career, Starting a Business, and Estate Planning) were filled through direct review of current first-party product and government sources on July 20, 2026. Update this document, not just the underlying research, whenever a guide's competitive position changes.

## Early Career

**What Exists in the Market:** Federal Student Aid's [Loan Simulator](https://studentaid.gov/loan-simulator/) compares federal repayment plans, projected payments, payoff dates, and potential forgiveness. Employer-benefit providers and general personal-finance sites separately offer paycheck, retirement-contribution, and apartment-affordability calculators.

**What's Missing:** These tools solve isolated decisions. The market reviewed does not connect a first offer and employer benefits, student-loan repayment, the true cost of moving out, and the first emergency-fund target into one early-career sequence using the same household data.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** Krovos connects First Job Decisions, the Student Loan Repayment Planner, and the First Apartment Calculator to the user's Life Graph. The differentiation is the coordinated life-phase path: compensation and benefits choices feed the debt, housing, and safety-net decisions that follow instead of producing three unrelated calculator results.

## Newlywed

**What Exists in the Market:** Budgeting apps with joint-account features (Monarch, YNAB) let couples see combined spending, and generic personal-finance articles cover "how to combine finances after marriage" at a conceptual level. Wedding-planning sites separately offer budget spreadsheets and vendor cost guides, treated as an entirely different topic from the financial-partnership decisions that follow the wedding itself.

**What's Missing:** No end-to-end flow covering legal name changes, beneficiary updates, and joint financial structure decisions together. Every existing resource treats the wedding budget, the account-structure decision (combined, separate, or hybrid), and post-wedding administrative tasks as three separate topics, requiring a couple to piece together their own sequence and often missing the administrative half entirely once the wedding itself is over.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** An integrated flow covering wedding budget, joint-account model decision, and post-wedding administrative tasks (beneficiary updates, wills, insurance) as one guide. The research confirms no competitor connects these three phases into a single guided lifecycle, a couple using existing tools would need a budgeting app, a wedding site, and a generic "newlywed checklist" to cover what this guide handles as one continuous decision.

## Home Buying

**What Exists in the Market:** Strong standalone rent-vs-buy calculators exist (NerdWallet and similar), generally a single-scenario tool that outputs a break-even year given a fixed set of assumptions. Separately, down-payment savings calculators exist, and mortgage-affordability calculators exist, each as its own isolated tool.

**What's Missing:** No integrated flow connecting rent-vs-buy economics, down-payment savings timeline, and life-fit factors. Existing calculators treat rent-vs-buy as a one-time, single-scenario question and don't model how long it actually takes a specific user to reach a target down payment given their real income and goals, or how sensitive the rent-vs-buy conclusion is to the holding period actually chosen.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** Three-horizon sensitivity testing (short, expected, and long holding periods, run side by side rather than one at a time) combined with an explicit life-fit override framework, tied directly to the user's real Life Graph data rather than generic manual inputs. This combination, testing the same decision under multiple realistic timelines using the user's actual numbers instead of a single calculator session, is confirmed absent elsewhere.

## Career Transition

**What Exists in the Market:** SeveranceCalc.net models severance, tax, and COBRA together and is the closest single competitor found. Mainstream platforms (Fidelity, SoFi) publish article-level guidance on job transitions, covering topics like negotiating severance or comparing offers, but without an interactive model behind the advice.

**What's Missing:** Nothing integrates severance, cash-flow runway, and ACA/COBRA policy decisions in one model. No tool accounts for the 2025-2026 ACA premium tax credit repayment-risk changes as part of a job-loss plan, a real and current gap since that policy shift directly affects marketplace coverage cost during a gap in employment.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** The equity-discounting framework (public vs. private company, vesting timeline discount applied at different rates) paired with the runway calculation, confirmed absent from every competitor reviewed. No other tool distinguishes how much an equity offer is actually worth based on company status and vest timing while simultaneously modeling the income gap a transition creates.

## College Planning

**What Exists in the Market:** Savingforcollege.com maintains a deep 529 tool suite covering state plans and projections, and generally does this piece well on its own. NerdWallet and Voya each offer a decent FAFSA/Student Aid Index calculator, generally treated as a one-time estimate rather than an ongoing planning input revisited as a family's income or household composition changes year to year.

**What's Missing:** Nothing connects 529 savings, FAFSA/SAI strategy, and loan decisions as one model across multiple years of a child's life. The blended-family stepparent-income FAFSA rule (a new spouse's income counts against a stepchild's aid eligibility regardless of any legal support obligation) is treated as a footnote everywhere it appears, not a first-class planning input worth modeling explicitly.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** The blended-family FAFSA mechanic integrated directly into planning rather than just explained in passing, letting a user actually model the aid impact of a custodial parent's remarriage instead of reading about the rule in the abstract and having to do the math themselves.

## Gig Work

**What Exists in the Market:** NerdWallet and dedicated apps (GigTax, Freelancer Tax Estimator) offer solid standalone self-employment tax calculators, generally built around a single quarter's estimated payment rather than the income pattern across a full year. A separate category of expense-tracking apps (Everlance, Hurdlr) handles mileage and deduction logging well but stops at recordkeeping.

**What's Missing:** Nothing combines income volatility modeling, continuous tax tracking, and benefits-replacement planning in one flow. No tool translates a user's actual historical income into a volatility-aware buffer recommendation, each existing tool handles one slice (taxes, or income tracking, or benefits) in isolation from the others.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** The baseline/ceiling/floor income framework combined with quarterly tax and benefits planning in a single guide. This specific combination, income volatility modeling plus ongoing tax obligations plus the benefits a W-2 job would have otherwise provided, is confirmed as a combination no competitor offers as one connected tool.

## Starting a Business

**What Exists in the Market:** The IRS provides authoritative but separate resources for [starting a business](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/starting-a-business), federal tax obligations, and [retirement plans for small entities and self-employed people](https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/retirement-plans-for-small-entities-and-self-employed). Bookkeeping products focus on transaction records, invoicing, expenses, and tax preparation.

**What's Missing:** The reviewed resources do not model the founder's W-2 job, household runway, business cash flow, estimated taxes, entity choice, and self-employed retirement options as one transition decision. Compliance information exists, but the point at which the business can safely replace employment income remains a manual judgment assembled across several systems.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** Revenue Target connects household runway and benefits replacement to business revenue using Life Graph data, while the Business Structure Comparison, Quarterly Tax Calculator, and Retirement Plan Comparison handle the operating decisions around it. Krovos differentiates on the transition as a household financial milestone, not on bookkeeping or tax filing.

## Divorce

**What Exists in the Market:** DivorceSmart and Resolvy offer bundled settlement and support calculators covering asset division scenarios and support payment estimates.

**What's Missing:** QDRO handling is treated as "talk to your attorney" rather than a modeled decision anywhere in the market reviewed. No consumer tool connects settlement modeling to post-divorce income rebuilding, or visualizes retirement outcomes under different QDRO or settlement structures, leaving a real gap between the legal process and the financial planning that follows it, one that generally goes unaddressed until well after the divorce is finalized.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** QDRO timing-risk framing (an awarded share can become difficult or impossible to recover if the order isn't finalized before the plan participant retires, dies, or rolls the account into a new plan) paired with the single-income rebuilding path in the same guide. Confirmed absent elsewhere: no competitor treats QDRO timing as an urgent, modelable financial risk rather than a legal afterthought handled separately from the money.

## Caregiving

**What Exists in the Market:** Long-term-care cost comparison tools and tax-credit explainers exist as separate, standalone resources, generally aimed at helping a family estimate what a parent's care will cost. AARP and Genworth both publish solid regional cost-of-care data, useful as a reference point but not built into any interactive planning tool.

**What's Missing:** Nothing integrates cost-of-care scenarios with the caregiver's own retirement and career trajectory. No tool quantifies the opportunity cost of reduced work hours against long-term retirement impact, the caregiver's own financial exposure is treated as a separate topic from the parent's care costs, if it is addressed at all in the resources reviewed.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** Caregiving Retirement Impact paired with practical cost-tracking (the Caregiver Cost Tracker) in the same guide, addressing exactly the integration gap the research confirms is missing industry-wide: what caregiving costs the parent, and what it costs the caregiver's own future, modeled together rather than as two unconnected concerns.

## Disability

**What Exists in the Market:** Advisor-grade tools (Snap Projections) model disability, critical illness, and life insurance together with real depth, but are built for financial advisors, not available to consumers directly. Consumer-facing tools reviewed are single-purpose benefit calculators covering one policy type at a time, and none walk a user through how a diagnosis actually changes month-to-month cash flow.

**What's Missing:** No consumer-accessible tool models benefit coordination and offset (LTD benefit reduced dollar-for-dollar by an SSDI award) with real numbers. This calculation exists in advisor software but nowhere a consumer can run it directly against their own policy terms and benefit estimates, leaving most people guessing at their actual combined monthly income during a claim.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** The Disability Benefits Calculator's month-by-month offset modeling, bringing advisor-grade depth (the actual LTD-minus-SSDI math, sequenced across a real disability timeline including waiting periods and approval delays) into a tool a consumer can use without an advisor relationship.

## Widowhood

**What Exists in the Market:** The SSA's own calculators are actuarially correct but widely reported as difficult to use, particularly for a survivor trying to compare claiming ages while grieving. Specialized grief-support sites (Widowed Community and similar) maintain strong task checklists but offer no interactive financial tools alongside them.

**What's Missing:** Nothing combines urgency-triaged task sequencing (what genuinely cannot wait vs. what can safely be deferred) with real survivor-benefit calculation in one place. The two categories of resource, task checklists and benefit calculators, exist independently and are never presented together, forcing a grieving user to reconcile them on their own at the worst possible time.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** The phased first-days/first-weeks/first-months structure combined with the Qualifying Surviving Spouse tax-status explanation (a favorable filing status available for two years after the year of death, on top of the year of death itself), a combination rarely covered this clearly anywhere per the research, most resources mention QSS status in passing if at all.

## Blended Family

**What Exists in the Market:** A small number of niche calculators visualize inheritance dilution risk in blended families, generally as a standalone exercise disconnected from any specific estate-planning decision. Generic estate-planning platforms (Trust & Will and similar) generate documents but don't surface blended-family-specific conflict points before a user starts drafting.

**What's Missing:** No mainstream tool offers a plain-language QTIP-vs-bypass trust decision guide tied specifically to blended-family goals. Nothing connects remarriage timing to FAFSA impact for stepchildren, these are treated as entirely separate topics (estate planning and college financial aid) everywhere else in the market reviewed, even though both are triggered by the same remarriage decision.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** The QTIP/bypass walkthrough paired directly with the FAFSA stepparent-income mechanic in the same guide, addressing a combination (estate structure decisions and college aid impact, both triggered by the same remarriage) that no competitor covers together, despite both stemming from the identical life event.

## Estate Planning

**What Exists in the Market:** Online estate-planning platforms such as [Trust & Will](https://trustandwill.com/) guide consumers through creating wills and trusts, beneficiary decisions, guardianship, and optional attorney support. Their core product is legal-document generation and maintenance.

**What's Missing:** Document platforms do not generally begin with a coordinated financial-readiness view across account beneficiaries, estate-tax exposure, life-insurance liquidity, and blended-family structure before routing the user to the appropriate legal help. Krovos has not yet decided whether it will ever draft documents, so document creation is not claimed as a competitive advantage.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** Krovos's defensible current position is organize, model, and refer: the Beneficiary Checker, Estate Tax Checker, Life Insurance Calculator, and QTIP vs. Bypass Comparison expose planning gaps using Life Graph data, then direct the user to qualified legal support for drafting. This claim must be revisited if Krovos chooses the unresolved document-drafting strategy.

## Inheritance

**What Exists in the Market:** Large brokerages maintain operational inherited-IRA workflows for their own account holders, generally limited to processing the mechanics of a transfer. Consumer content on windfalls generally is generic rule-of-thumb advice, commonly a version of the "1/3 rule" (a third to debt, a third to savings, a third to investing).

**What's Missing:** Step-up in basis is explained in general terms everywhere but never turned into an actual decision tool (sell now vs. hold, given the specific appraised value and current market). The distinction between a spouse, an eligible designated beneficiary, and a standard 10-year-rule beneficiary, each with genuinely different distribution requirements, is often oversimplified or stated incorrectly in the consumer content reviewed.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** Precise beneficiary-category-based distribution guidance paired with an explicit warning against taking an immediate lump-sum distribution, since that decision cannot be undone once made. This precision, correctly distinguishing which rules apply to which beneficiary type, is confirmed missing from the generic advice that dominates this topic.

## Retirement

**What Exists in the Market:** Advanced tools (ProjectionLab, Boldin) handle Monte Carlo retirement simulation well and are generally considered the strongest consumer-facing retirement modeling available.

**What's Missing:** CoastFI is rarely modeled as a first-class milestone in competing tools, typically buried as a secondary output of a full retirement projection rather than its own tracked goal. WEP/GPO modeling is almost universally absent or outdated given the 2025 repeal, most tools still assume WEP/GPO benefit reductions apply to pension holders who also qualify for Social Security, when that reduction is no longer current law.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** Correcting stale WEP/GPO assumptions with the 2025 repeal explicitly addressed, confirmed as a real, current gap in competing tools, several of which still model a benefit reduction that no longer exists. This is a factual currency gap, not just a feature gap: someone relying on another tool's output could be planning around a benefit cut that was already repealed.

## Immigration

**What Exists in the Market:** Niche tools (Boundless-style visa cost roundups, remittance comparison apps like Wise and Remitly) each cover one narrow slice of the immigration financial picture well. Immigration attorneys' own client resources cover legal timelines competently but rarely touch the financial planning that runs alongside them.

**What's Missing:** Mainstream financial planning platforms don't model visa-status risk in financial planning at all, it simply doesn't appear as an input anywhere in general-purpose tools. No tool integrates work-authorization timeline risk (the gap between a visa's authorization status and actual income availability) with financial buffer planning for the household affected by it.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** Explicit modeling of income planning during authorization gaps, paired with portable retirement account guidance (what actually happens to 401k and IRA balances if the account holder leaves the US), confirmed absent from every competitor reviewed. No other tool treats immigration status itself as a direct financial planning input.

## New Parent

**What Exists in the Market:** Fidelity publishes the closest holistic "new child" guide found in the market, covering benefits, leave, and savings at a general level. Baby-registry and parenting sites (BabyCenter, The Bump) publish cost estimates for gear and childcare but treat these as one-time shopping guidance, not ongoing financial planning.

**What's Missing:** No tool models paid-leave income gaps for the roughly 42 states without a state paid family leave program, coverage in every resource reviewed concentrates almost entirely on California's SDI/PFL system as if it were universal. Childcare cost planning across the market is universally editorial content (articles citing national averages), not an interactive calculation against a user's actual location and situation.

**Krovos Differentiation Claim:** The HDHP vs. PPO birth-year worst-case cost formula, paired with the non-California birth-recovery funding framework (modeling the income gap in the large majority of states with no state paid leave program), a combination confirmed absent from every competitor reviewed, most of which implicitly assume California-equivalent coverage exists everywhere.
