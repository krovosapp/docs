## What This Guide Is

Estate planning sounds like a project for the wealthy or the elderly. In practice, it is a small number of specific documents that anyone with a dependent, a home, or an opinion about who should raise their children needs to have in place. This guide covers what those documents actually are, why the most common misconception (that this is primarily about avoiding estate tax) does not apply to most people, and what to do about it now rather than eventually.

---

## Part 1: The Documents That Actually Do the Work

### The will

A will directs how your assets are distributed and, critically, names a guardian for minor children. Without a will, your state's default inheritance laws decide who receives your assets, and a court decides who raises your children if both parents are gone. Neither of those defaults is likely to match what you would actually choose.

### Beneficiary designations

Retirement accounts, life insurance policies, and many bank and brokerage accounts pass directly to whoever is named as beneficiary, regardless of what your will says. This is one of the most commonly overlooked pieces of estate planning: a will naming your spouse means nothing if your 401k beneficiary is still listed as an ex-partner from a decade ago.

**Confirm beneficiary designations on every account, especially after any major life change:** marriage, divorce, birth of a child, or death of a previously named beneficiary.

### Do you need a trust

For most people, the answer is no, a will and correct beneficiary designations cover the situation. Trusts become genuinely useful when you want to avoid probate for privacy or speed, when you have a blended family with competing interests to balance, when you have a dependent with special needs who requires specific financial protections, or when your estate is large enough that estate tax planning is relevant.

### What this actually costs

A basic attorney-drafted will typically runs $300 to $1,000, more in large metro areas or with added complexity. Online platforms offering a basic will run roughly $70 to $250, with bundles including power of attorney and a healthcare directive in the $170 to $550 range. A full attorney-drafted comprehensive plan, more relevant if you need a trust or have a complicated situation, commonly runs $1,500 to $4,000. Online or DIY options are reasonable when your estate is modest, your assets are straightforward, and your beneficiaries are clear; an attorney is worth the added cost when you have a blended family, a business, a beneficiary with special needs, or any real risk of dispute among heirs.

### Federal estate tax: probably not your issue

As of 2026, the federal estate tax exemption is $15,000,000 per person, or $30,000,000 for a married couple, with portability between spouses (2026 figure confirmed: IRS.gov estate tax filing threshold table; figure is indexed annually for inflation -- verify the current figure with an estate attorney before making irrevocable planning decisions). Only estates above this threshold owe any federal estate tax. For the significant majority of people, federal estate tax is not the reason to do estate planning. The reasons that actually apply to most people are guardianship, avoiding probate delays, and making sure assets go where intended, not tax minimization.

**A note on recent legislative history:** The elevated estate tax exemption originally created by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 was scheduled to sunset at the end of 2025 and revert to approximately $5 million per person (adjusted for inflation). The One Big Beautiful Bill (Public Law 119-21), signed July 4, 2025, addressed that scheduled sunset and established the 2026 federal estate tax exemption at $15,000,000 per person. This was confirmed in the IRS tax year 2026 inflation adjustments release. If you are doing significant estate planning involving large wealth transfers, work with a qualified estate attorney to confirm the current figure, since this area of law changed materially in 2025.

### The community property flag

If you have ever lived in a community property state while married, your spouse may have a legal claim to part of your retirement accounts regardless of what your beneficiary designation actually says. This is a real, commonly overlooked exception to the general rule that a beneficiary form controls the outcome, and it is worth a direct conversation with an attorney if this applies to your situation, rather than assuming your paperwork settles it.

## Part 2: Who's in Charge If You Can't Be

### The authority gap a will does not fill

A will only takes effect after death. It says nothing about who makes decisions if you are alive but incapacitated, whether from an accident, an illness, or a temporary medical event. Without a separate document, that decision can end up in front of a court.

**Durable financial power of attorney** names someone to manage your financial affairs if you cannot. **Healthcare power of attorney**, sometimes combined with a healthcare directive or living will, names someone to make medical decisions and states your own wishes about care.

These can be the same person or different people, depending on who you trust with which kind of decision. Confirm whoever you name has actually agreed to the role. Naming someone without asking them is a common and avoidable gap.

## Part 3: Guardianship, If You Have Children

If something happened to both parents, a court decides who raises your children unless your will says otherwise. The court does not know your reasoning, your family dynamics, or who you would have actually chosen. It will make a decision based on limited information and legal defaults.

**Naming a guardian in your will is the only way to make this your decision instead of a court's.** Have the direct conversation with the person you are considering before naming them. A guardian who has not agreed to the role, or who is unaware they have been named, is not a real plan.

The death of a spouse who is named as a guardian or executor in your own estate documents is a review trigger requiring immediate, not eventual, attention. A document that names a deceased person in either role creates administrative delay and confusion, not a plan that can be carried out.

## Part 4: What Changes When Life Changes

Estate documents reflect the life you had when you wrote them, not necessarily the life you have now. A will written before a marriage, a divorce, a new child, or a significant change in assets does not update itself.

**Review triggers:** marriage, divorce, birth or adoption of a child, death of a named beneficiary or guardian, a significant change in assets, or moving to a new state, since some estate laws vary by state.

**A note on divorce specifically:** updating beneficiary designations on financial accounts after a divorce is necessary but not sufficient. A divorce decree does not automatically remove an ex-spouse who is named as your executor or as your children's guardian in your estate documents. Neither role is vacated by the divorce itself. After a divorce, a full review of your will, executor designation, guardian designation, and power of attorney is a separate and necessary step from the beneficiary form updates.

The gift tax annual exclusion for 2026 is $19,000 per recipient, or $38,000 for a married couple gifting jointly (2026 figure confirmed: IRS tax year 2026 inflation adjustments release and Rev. Proc. 2025-32; unchanged from 2025; indexed annually for inflation and subject to change in subsequent years). You can give this amount to any number of people each year without it counting against the lifetime exemption or requiring a gift tax filing. This is relevant primarily for those doing deliberate lifetime gifting as part of a larger estate strategy, not for most people's basic estate planning needs.

## Part 5: Blended Families

### Where default assumptions go wrong

Standard estate planning assumptions, that everything passes to a spouse, then to children, often do not produce the outcome a blended family actually wants. Without specific planning, a surviving spouse could inherit everything, leaving children from a prior relationship with no guaranteed inheritance if the surviving spouse's own estate plan does not include them.

**A common tool for this situation is a trust that provides for a surviving spouse during their lifetime while preserving the remaining assets for children from a prior relationship after the surviving spouse's death.** This requires deliberate drafting; it does not happen automatically through a standard will.

**Life insurance can also solve this cleanly in simpler situations:** naming children from a prior relationship directly as life insurance beneficiaries guarantees they receive a specific inheritance, independent of what happens with the rest of the estate.

Have the explicit conversation with your spouse about what "fair" means for your specific blended family. There is no universal right answer, but leaving it undiscussed and unplanned is how blended families end up in prolonged conflict after a death.

## Part 6: Starting the Conversation With Family

### Opening the topic with a resistant parent

Frame it as care and preparation, not a takeover: "I want to make sure we can honor what you want if something happens" keeps the focus on their autonomy, not your agenda. Choose a calm, low-stakes moment rather than a health scare, sometimes a news story or a friend's situation is a natural, non-threatening opening. Start with logistics, what exists and where it's kept, rather than dollar amounts, which reduces the sense that you're prying into their finances and keeps the conversation about preparedness, not inheritance.

### Telling adult children your decisions in advance

Share the broad outlines of your plan and, importantly, the reasoning behind it. Family members who understand why a particular executor was chosen, or why an asset was divided a certain way, are far less likely to contest the plan or feel blindsided later. This applies especially to anything that could look unequal on its surface, a shared property, a specific sentimental item, explaining the reasoning while you're still able to is far better than leaving survivors to guess. Treat this as an ongoing conversation you can revisit as circumstances change, not a single disclosure you make once and never repeat.

## Action Plan

**If you have no will:**
- [ ] Start one this week, even a basic one covering asset distribution and guardianship
- [ ] Name a guardian for any minor children and confirm they have agreed to the role
- [ ] Name a durable financial power of attorney and a healthcare power of attorney

**If you have an existing will:**
- [ ] Pull it out and check the date against your current life circumstances
- [ ] Confirm the named guardian, if applicable, is still the right choice and still willing
- [ ] Review beneficiary designations on every retirement account, life insurance policy, and applicable bank account

**Ongoing:**
- [ ] Revisit all estate documents after any marriage, divorce, birth, death, or major asset change
- [ ] Confirm your named power of attorney and healthcare proxy are still the right people and still willing
