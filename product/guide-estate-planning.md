# Estate Planning

## The outcome

This upgrade does not draft legal documents. It gives the member a complete readiness inventory, beneficiary audit, secure document map, decision record, and attorney handoff packet using information already in Krovos. It asks only for missing or changed facts and requires no new AI report.

## Confirm people and authority

Krovos records the people the member may want to serve as guardian, personal representative or executor, trustee, financial agent, health-care agent, and digital-asset contact. It stores alternates, location, and discussion status—not a legal appointment. State-specific documents and execution rules require qualified counsel or an approved legal service.

## Build the estate inventory

The inventory covers titled property, financial accounts, retirement plans, insurance, businesses, debts, digital assets, personal property, expected inheritances, and dependent or pet needs. Every item records owner, title, beneficiary or transfer mechanism, approximate value, document location, and last verification date.

## Run the beneficiary and ownership audit

The Beneficiary Checker compares the member's stated intention with the recorded title and beneficiary designation. It flags missing, outdated, inconsistent, contingent, minor-child, special-needs, former-partner, and deceased-beneficiary scenarios for confirmation. It does not claim that a will controls every asset or automatically instruct the member to change a designation.

## Plan for a beneficiary with disabilities

When the Beneficiary Checker identifies a person with disabilities or a beneficiary who receives means-tested benefits, Krovos opens this branch instead of leaving the result as a warning. The purpose is to preserve care continuity, prepare the family for a specialized legal conversation, and prevent a well-intended gift or inheritance from creating an avoidable benefits problem.

### Protect public-benefit eligibility

SSI and many Medicaid pathways use income and resource rules. An inheritance, direct cash gift, beneficiary designation, or account titled in the beneficiary's name may become countable and interrupt eligibility. Krovos asks who currently receives SSI or Medicaid, what assets or beneficiary designations could pass directly to that person, and which family members may also be planning gifts. It then adds every potential direct transfer to the attorney handoff packet.

Krovos does not tell the member to move or retitle assets on its own. Benefit treatment depends on the program, the state, the source of the funds, and the legal structure. The action is to pause an unreviewed direct transfer and coordinate the estate plan with a special-needs planning attorney and the beneficiary's benefits specialist.

### Understand where an ABLE account fits

An ABLE account is a tax-advantaged account owned by an eligible person with a disability and used for qualified disability expenses. Beginning in 2026, the disability must have begun before age 46. The 2026 standard contribution limit is $20,000 across contributors. An eligible employed beneficiary may be able to contribute an additional amount under ABLE-to-Work rules. Krovos records the member-confirmed account, beneficiary, state program, contribution history, and intended expenses without treating the account as a substitute for a trust.

For SSI, up to $100,000 in an ABLE account is excluded from the resource calculation. Amounts above that level can suspend SSI when they cause countable resources to exceed the applicable limit, while Medicaid may continue if the person remains otherwise eligible. Distribution rules also matter, particularly for housing and nonqualified expenses. Krovos therefore presents the current federal thresholds as reviewable facts and routes account-specific decisions to the ABLE program, benefits specialist, and tax professional.

### Distinguish first-party and third-party trusts

A first-party special-needs trust holds assets that belong to the beneficiary, such as a settlement or inheritance already received. It must meet specific federal and state requirements and generally includes Medicaid payback provisions at the beneficiary's death.

A third-party special-needs trust holds assets contributed by someone else, often a parent or grandparent through an estate plan. When properly drafted and administered, it can supplement the beneficiary's life without making the trust assets available to the beneficiary as a countable resource. It generally does not require Medicaid payback, so the document can direct remaining assets according to the grantor's plan.

Krovos does not select or draft either trust. It identifies whose money is involved, whether assets have already reached the beneficiary, how future gifts and beneficiary designations are written, and which questions counsel must resolve.

### Choose a trustee for this specific responsibility

The trustee of a special-needs trust needs more than general financial reliability. The role may require understanding benefit rules, permitted distributions, tax and recordkeeping duties, care providers, housing arrangements, family dynamics, and the beneficiary's preferences over many years. Krovos helps the member compare an individual trustee, a professional or corporate trustee, and a co-trustee structure across continuity, expertise, relationship, cost, conflicts, and succession.

The trustee decision remains separate from the choice of guardian, health-care agent, representative payee, or care coordinator. One person may hold more than one role, but Krovos never assumes that the best caregiver is also the best long-term trustee.

### Write the care knowledge that legal documents cannot hold

A letter of intent records the practical knowledge another caregiver would need if the current parent or caregiver could no longer provide guidance. It may cover the beneficiary's routines, communication style, relationships, education, work, health care, medications, providers, housing, transportation, sensory needs, preferences, sources of comfort, public benefits, and the family's hopes for the future.

The letter is guidance, not a binding legal document. Krovos gives it a named owner and review date, stores its location in the secure document map, and prompts a review after changes in health, school, work, housing, benefits, caregivers, or family structure.

### Make the specialized handoff

This branch requires a lawyer whose regular practice includes special-needs planning and coordination with SSI and Medicaid, not only a general estate-planning practice. The handoff packet includes:

- Current benefits and the state administering them
- Existing ABLE accounts, trusts, representative-payee arrangements, and guardianship or supported-decision-making documents
- Assets already owned by the beneficiary
- Wills, trusts, accounts, policies, and relatives' plans that may transfer assets directly
- Proposed trustees, successor trustees, care coordinators, and other decision-makers
- The letter of intent and its review owner
- Questions about trust type, payback, distributions, taxes, housing, benefit reporting, and state-specific rules

The Beneficiary Checker points here when it detects the issue. This branch sends the member back to the checker after counsel provides instructions, so each account and policy can be verified against the completed plan rather than managed as a separate list.

## Decide what counsel needs to solve

Krovos screens for minor children, blended families, unmarried partners, disability or benefits needs, business ownership, real estate in multiple states, foreign property or citizenship, likely conflict, charitable goals, privacy needs, and potential estate or inheritance tax exposure. Each positive answer becomes an attorney question.

The Estate Tax Checker is a screening tool only. It uses a verified current federal threshold and member-confirmed state rules, labels estimates and missing inputs, and never applies a flat rate as an owed-tax calculation. Portability, basis, gifting, trusts, and tax elections go to an estate attorney and tax professional.

The QTIP vs. Bypass Comparison remains an educational handoff tool. It may compare goals and tradeoffs, but it does not select a trust or present a structure as a strong fit.

## Create the secure document map

Krovos stores a map—not secret keys or document contents unless the member intentionally uploads them—showing where the will, trusts, powers of attorney, health directives, deeds, policies, business documents, tax records, and instructions can be found. The plan records who knows how to access them and what should never be placed in an ordinary note.

## The attorney handoff packet

The packet contains household and dependent facts, inventory, beneficiary conflicts, named-role candidates, state and property connections, goals, open questions, existing documents, and decisions the member has not made. After the meeting, the member records counsel's instructions and execution requirements rather than Krovos interpreting documents.

## The 90-day plan

- **Now:** inventory, dependents, current documents, urgent beneficiary conflicts, and access risks.
- **30 days:** decisions and attorney questions; schedule appropriate professional help.
- **60 days:** draft and review through the chosen provider; verify titles and designations separately.
- **90 days:** execute correctly, store the document map, notify responsible people, and assign reviews.

Annual reminders and life-event triggers reopen only affected sections after marriage, divorce, birth, death, relocation, business changes, large asset changes, or a change in intended decision-makers.

## Sources and review standard

Verify current federal tax and ABLE contribution figures with the IRS; SSI, ABLE resource, and trust treatment with the Social Security Administration; Medicaid treatment with the relevant state Medicaid agency; retirement and benefit rules with plan administrators; and all trust, document, probate, guardianship, supported-decision-making, property, and state-tax questions with qualified state-specific professionals. Review official source dates before publication and at least annually.
