## What This Guide Is

Inherited money arrives because of a loss, often while you are still grieving, and the pressure to decide what to do with it immediately is rarely coming from an actual deadline. This guide covers what genuinely needs attention right away, what does not, and how to eventually integrate an inheritance into your financial life without letting either grief or excitement drive the decision.

---

## Part 1: The Decision You Don't Have to Rush

Most financial decisions related to an inheritance can wait until you are actually ready to think clearly. A small number of specific items do have real deadlines. The goal of this section is separating the two, so you are not treating everything as urgent when most of it is not, and not missing the few things that genuinely are.

---

## Part 2: What You Actually Inherited

### Cash and taxable accounts

Inherited cash and most taxable investment accounts generally receive a "step-up in basis" to the value at the date of death, meaning if you sell inherited investments soon after receiving them, there is often little to no capital gains tax owed, since the cost basis reset to current value rather than what the original owner paid decades earlier.

### Inherited retirement accounts: the genuine deadline

This is the area with real, binding rules, and it's more nuanced than a single blanket deadline. Beneficiaries fall into different categories with different rules.

**Most beneficiaries, called non-eligible designated beneficiaries, must fully distribute an inherited IRA or 401k within 10 years of the original owner's death.** This covers most adult children and most people inheriting from someone who isn't their spouse.

**A specific set of "eligible designated beneficiaries" get more flexible treatment**: a surviving spouse, a minor child of the original owner (only while still a minor), someone disabled or chronically ill under the IRS definition, or someone not more than 10 years younger than the original owner. These beneficiaries can generally take distributions based on their own life expectancy instead of the strict 10-year rule, at least initially. A minor child's status changes once they're no longer legally a minor, at which point the 10-year clock starts.

**Whether you owe distributions every year during that 10-year window, not just a lump sum at the end, depends on whether the original owner had already reached their own required distribution age at death.** If they died before reaching that age, you can generally wait and take the full amount any time within the 10 years, including all at once in year 10. If they died after reaching that age, you generally owe annual distributions throughout the 10 years, calculated based on your own life expectancy, in addition to fully emptying the account by the end of year 10.

**Inherited Roth IRAs get a meaningfully better deal**: the 10-year rule still applies, but there is generally no requirement for annual distributions within that window, since Roth accounts never carry a required distribution age for the original owner. You can let a Roth grow tax-free for the full 10 years and take everything at the end if that suits your tax situation.

**Spouses have the most flexible options of all,** including treating an inherited IRA as their own, which resets the rules to standard IRA treatment rather than the 10-year rule.

**Do not take an immediate lump-sum distribution while sorting out grief or logistics.** Once you take money out of an inherited retirement account, that decision cannot be undone, there is no way to roll it back into an inherited IRA afterward. Leave the funds in place and confirm your beneficiary category and the applicable rules before touching anything.

Every dollar distributed from an inherited traditional IRA or 401k is taxed as ordinary income in the year you take it. This makes timing genuinely consequential: taking a large distribution in a high-income year costs meaningfully more in tax than spreading distributions across lower-income years within the 10-year window.

### Inherited property

Inherited real estate also generally receives a step-up in basis to date-of-death value. If you plan to sell, get a qualified appraisal near the time of death to document that value clearly for tax purposes.

## Part 3: How This Changes Your Own Plan

An inheritance is not meant to sit permanently separate from the rest of your financial life. Once you are ready, and only once you are ready, it is worth revisiting your existing debt payoff plan, your retirement trajectory, and any major goals like a home purchase with this inheritance factored in.

There is no requirement to decide this immediately, and there is real value in waiting until grief has settled enough that you are making a financial decision, not an emotional one. The exception is the inherited retirement account timeline above, which continues running regardless of your readiness to engage with it.

## Part 4: The Tax and Legal Details Easy to Miss

### Estate tax is rarely the issue

As of 2026, the federal estate tax exemption is $15 million per person (2026 figure, indexed annually for inflation -- verify the current figure with a tax professional for planning decisions). The estate itself, not you as the beneficiary, would be responsible for any federal estate tax, and only estates above this threshold owe any. For the significant majority of inheritances, federal estate tax is not a relevant concern for the person receiving the inheritance.

### State-level considerations

A small number of states impose their own inheritance or estate tax with much lower thresholds than the federal exemption. Confirm whether the state where the deceased lived, not necessarily where you live, has its own inheritance or estate tax that could apply.

### When to get professional guidance

If the inheritance includes a retirement account with required distribution timelines, a business interest, or a total value large enough that tax strategy meaningfully matters, a conversation with a tax professional is worth the cost, particularly around how to sequence retirement account distributions across the 10-year window in the most tax-efficient way for your specific income situation.

## Part 5: Inheriting a House Specifically

### The three real options, weighed honestly

**Move in:** Only makes sense if the home genuinely fits your life, not out of obligation or nostalgia. Factor in the real cost of any deferred maintenance, and whether the home's location and size fit your actual needs, not the needs of the person who lived there.

**Rent it out:** Turns the inheritance into ongoing income but also ongoing responsibility: tenant management, maintenance, insurance, and the tax complexity of rental property. If multiple siblings inherited the property jointly, rental decisions require everyone's ongoing agreement, which can become a source of conflict if priorities differ.

**Sell it:** The most common choice, and often the simplest, particularly when multiple heirs are involved and a physical asset cannot be easily divided. Since inherited property generally receives a step-up in basis to date-of-death value, a sale soon after inheriting typically generates little to no taxable gain.

### If siblings inherited jointly

Get an independent appraisal before any decision, so all parties are working from the same, neutral valuation rather than differing assumptions about what the home is worth. Decide the process for disagreement in advance, such as a supermajority vote or a buyout option for any sibling who wants to keep the property while others want to sell, rather than discovering there is no process once a disagreement actually arises.

## Part 6: When It's Not Just About Money

### Dividing sentimental items without a fight

Fights over possessions are rarely about the object's value, they're about identity, history, and feeling seen. A structured process helps more than trying to be perfectly fair: hold a dedicated conversation just about personal items, separate from money or property. Have each person privately list what matters most to them emotionally, then compare lists to see where interests actually overlap versus conflict. For items several people want, a simple turn-taking or category-based approach, one person gets first pick in one category, another in a different one, works better than litigating each item individually. Writing down who received what, and why, prevents the same disagreement from resurfacing years later.

### Permission to go slowly

Grief is a genuinely bad time for big, irreversible decisions, and you're allowed to treat inherited money and possessions as parked until you're actually ready to think clearly. A useful practice: separate truly time-sensitive tasks, like specific retirement account deadlines already covered, from everything else, and agree with family that non-urgent decisions simply wait for an agreed future date.

### When family wants you to decide faster than you're ready

Naming the grief directly, "we're all grieving, and that makes this harder, I want us to take the time to think clearly", can lower the temperature more than trying to out-argue urgency. If disagreement is significant, a neutral third party, not to decide who gets what but to keep the conversation constructive, is a legitimate resource, not a failure to handle it as family. When stuck, ask what the person you lost would have actually prioritized, peace between the people they loved, or a specific object, since that question often reframes what's really at stake.

## Action Plan

**Right away:**
- [ ] Identify whether you inherited a retirement account and confirm the specific distribution timeline that applies to you
- [ ] Confirm whether you are a spouse or non-spouse beneficiary, since the rules differ significantly
- [ ] Get a qualified appraisal on any inherited real estate near the date of death, if you plan to eventually sell

**When you are ready, with no forced timeline:**
- [ ] Revisit your debt, savings, and retirement plan with this inheritance factored in
- [ ] Decide how you want to sequence any required retirement account distributions across the 10-year window
- [ ] Confirm whether any state-level inheritance tax applies to your situation

**If the inheritance is complex:**
- [ ] Consult a tax professional specifically about distribution timing for inherited retirement accounts
- [ ] Consult an estate attorney if the inheritance includes a business interest or unusually complex assets
