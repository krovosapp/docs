## The outcome

An inheritance is a legal and tax process before it is a planning opportunity. This Guide separates executor work from beneficiary decisions, identifies genuine deadlines, and creates a saved timeline using the member's existing Krovos plan. It does not administer an estate, calculate a required distribution, interpret a trust, or replace legal or tax advice.

## Start with your role

### Executor, personal representative, or trustee

Your first responsibility is to the estate or trust and its governing documents—not an allocation for personal goals. Confirm appointment and authority, secure property and records, preserve insurance, identify creditors and deadlines, value assets, coordinate filings, keep estate funds separate, document transactions, and do not distribute assets prematurely. State law controls much of this process.

### Beneficiary only

Do not assume you can access, sell, retitle, disclaim, or distribute estate property. Request the governing information and administration timeline from the authorized person. Preserve every statement, valuation, tax form, and distribution letter.

If you hold both roles, keep fiduciary actions and personal decisions separate. A conflict, disputed interpretation, insolvent estate, business, trust, foreign asset, or pressure from another heir belongs with a qualified estate attorney.

## The first 30 days: preserve choices

- secure property, mail, accounts, digital access, insurance, and records through the authorized person;
- identify the will, trust, beneficiary designations, deed, titles, debts, and professional contacts;
- determine which assets pass through the estate and which pass by contract, title, or trust;
- ask each retirement custodian for the beneficiary record, account type, owner dates, year-of-death RMD status, and written options;
- request date-of-death values and qualified appraisals where needed;
- park received cash in an appropriately insured account while decisions are pending;
- do not commingle estate money, take a retirement distribution, retitle property, disclaim, or make gifts solely to simplify matters without understanding the consequence.

Not every decision can wait. A distribution, disclaimer, tax election, creditor notice, probate filing, insurance lapse, or property risk may have a deadline. Record the date only after the authorized professional or custodian confirms it.

## Build the inheritance inventory

For every asset, save the value; owner and recipient; probate, beneficiary, title, or trust path; date-of-death value and source; debt, lien, restriction, or expense; tax character; control status; responsible person; missing document; deadline; and professional question. This prevents treating every inherited dollar as interchangeable.

## Inherited retirement accounts: classify before modeling

Before Inherited IRA Planner unlocks a scenario, confirm:

1. traditional IRA, Roth IRA, or employer plan;
2. spouse, eligible designated beneficiary, other individual, or non-individual beneficiary;
3. whether a trust is named;
4. owner's date of death and required-beginning-date status;
5. whether the owner's year-of-death RMD was fully taken;
6. custodian or plan options and deadlines;
7. whether annual beneficiary RMDs apply in addition to an end-of-window deadline.

IRS Publication 590-B explains that rules vary by beneficiary and whether the owner died before or after the required beginning date. For many other individual beneficiaries, the account must be emptied by the end of year ten. If the owner died before the required beginning date and that rule applies, no distribution is required before year ten; other fact patterns can require annual distributions. Roth beneficiaries still face distribution rules even though the owner had no lifetime Roth IRA RMD.

Spouses have additional options, and employer plans can impose limits. Trusts, estates, charities, minor children, disabled or chronically ill beneficiaries, multiple beneficiaries, older deaths, and successor beneficiaries require separate treatment. Krovos routes these cases instead of improvising.

The Planner compares user-selected scenarios only after the rule path is confirmed. It does not label the lowest modeled federal tax “best,” because growth, state tax, Medicare premiums, credits, deductions, benefits, cash needs, and actual RMDs can change the result. Save the selected scenario and unresolved facts.

## Basis and inherited property

IRS Publication 559 states that inherited-property basis is generally fair market value at death, an alternate valuation if properly elected, or another applicable special value. Exceptions exist. Joint ownership, community property, depreciation, prior gifts, and legal ownership can change the calculation.

Use Step-Up in Basis only after saving the valuation source and confirmed ownership share. Selling costs, improvements, depreciation, state tax, net investment income tax, exclusions, and special rules remain open unless entered. Retirement accounts and other income-in-respect-of-a-decedent items do not receive this treatment.

For property, document the mortgage, liens, insurance, taxes, utilities, maintenance, occupancy, title, authority, appraisal, selling costs, co-owner process, and move-in, rent, sell, or hold scenarios. Price is only one factor.

## Taxes that may matter

Federal estate tax is an estate-level issue and not the only tax question. State estate or inheritance tax, estate/trust income tax, beneficiary income tax, capital gains, retirement distributions, business tax, foreign reporting, and income in respect of a decedent may apply. Relevant jurisdictions can include where the deceased lived, where property sits, where the estate is administered, and where the beneficiary lives.

Do not use a federal exemption headline as a substitute for an estate return or portability analysis. Ask which returns, elections, K-1s, basis statements, and dates apply.

## Integrate the inheritance without prescribing an answer

Inheritance Planner carries forward captured debt, cash runway, retirement facts, goals, and spending. The member drafts and saves allocations for taxes and unresolved liabilities; a protected holding reserve; urgent risk or high-cost debt; emergency and near-term needs; retirement and long-term goals; a values-based use; and an undecided amount.

The tool must not automatically send every remainder to a brokerage account or assume six months of spending, a 7% debt threshold, or one IRA contribution is right for everyone. Krovos shows tradeoffs; the member chooses the working allocation and review date.

## The saved decision timeline

### Now

- confirm role, authority, asset security, insurance, and urgent deadlines;
- preserve statements, valuations, documents, and correspondence;
- confirm year-of-death retirement distributions and property risk.

### Before accepting, disclaiming, distributing, retitling, or selling

- identify legal owner and transfer path;
- confirm beneficiary class and account rule;
- obtain basis and valuation documentation;
- identify taxes, debts, restrictions, and professional needs;
- record family or co-owner agreement.

### After distribution, when ready

- run the appropriate tool using verified inputs;
- choose and save a working scenario, not an automatic recommendation;
- update debt, cash, goals, retirement, insurance, estate, and tax plans;
- set 30-day, tax-season, and annual review dates.

## Family, grief, privacy, and fraud

Use a written process for sentimental property: private priority lists, neutral valuation where needed, an agreed selection method, and a final record. Keep financial conversations separate from grief conversations when possible. Verify professionals, custodians, payment instructions, and title requests through known channels; never send money or credentials from an unsolicited message.
