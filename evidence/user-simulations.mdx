## **About These Simulations**

These three simulations extend the Life Understanding Report format across different life phases and situations. Each uses a realistic composite profile. Financial figures and personal details are illustrative. The architecture, recognition quality, burden identification, and opportunity surfacing are what matter — these are the design standards every Life Understanding Report should meet.

The test for each simulation: does the person feel seen? Does the report reflect their specific situation, not a template? Does it open with recognition before moving to recommendation?

**USER SIMULATION 1**

**Maya, 26**

| **26** Age | **Foundation** Life Phase | *Single, early career, renting, carrying student debt, just started first **"**real**"** job with benefits* |
| --- | --- | --- |

| **Situation Summary** Single income: $58,000 gross / $3,820 take-home biweekly · Student loans: $34,000 federal / $12,000 private · 401k: newly enrolled at 3%, no employer match understanding · HSA-eligible HDHP plan selected at enrollment · Rent: $1,150/month · Emergency fund: $800 |
| --- |

**01  YOUR SITUATION**

Maya, you're 26 and doing something genuinely difficult: building a financial foundation from scratch on a starting salary, while carrying $46,000 in student loans that nobody prepared you to manage. You just enrolled in benefits for the first time — and made one choice that could cost you thousands if you're not careful about how you use it. But the pieces are in place to make real progress in the next 24 months. Here is what you're actually working with.

**02  FINANCIAL SNAPSHOT**

Take-home: $3,820/month · Student debt: $46,000 ($34K federal, $12K private) · Emergency fund: $800 (0.2 months of expenses) · Retirement: $0 saved + 3% contributions just started · Health: HDHP enrolled — HSA not yet opened · Net worth: approximately -$43,000

**03  WHAT YOU****'****RE CARRYING**

1. No emergency fund — 0.2 months of coverage against a standard 3-6 month target. Any unexpected expense becomes a credit card balance. Priority: build to $3,000 before accelerating debt payoff. 2. HSA eligible but not opened — you selected the HDHP, which qualifies you for an HSA. You have not opened one. Every month this account sits empty, you're leaving a tax advantage on the table. 3. Private student loans at [rate]% — these are not eligible for income-driven repayment or forgiveness. They should be prioritized over federal loans for payoff acceleration.

**04  YOUR OPPORTUNITIES**

HSA: Open an HSA today. Contribute the maximum — $4,150 for a single filer in 2026. Every dollar contributed is tax-free, grows tax-free, and withdraws tax-free for medical expenses. This is the only triple-tax-advantaged account that exists. · Student loan repayment: Your federal loans qualify for income-driven repayment — which at your income would reduce your monthly payment and potentially qualify you for forgiveness in 20-25 years. Your private loans do not qualify. The optimal strategy is different for each.

**05  WHAT TO DO FIRST**

1. Open an HSA — this week. Takes 15 minutes online. Set up a $100/month automatic contribution. 2. Build $1,000 emergency fund — pause extra debt payments until you have one month of expenses. 3. Call your federal loan servicer — ask to be enrolled in SAVE (Saving on a Valuable Education) repayment. This likely reduces your monthly federal loan payment.

**06  MOVING FORWARD**

You are not behind. You are at the beginning — and you have advantages that compound: time, an employer-sponsored retirement account you're already contributing to, and an HSA waiting to be activated. The next 24 months will set the trajectory for the next 20 years. The decisions you make now — particularly around the HSA and student loan repayment structure — will matter more than almost any financial decision you make in your thirties.

**USER SIMULATION 2**

**Robert ****&**** Sandra, 58 ****&**** 56**

| **58 ****&**** 56** Age | **Pre-Retirement** Life Phase | *Empty nesters, mortgage nearly paid off, highest income years, retirement 7 years away* |
| --- | --- | --- |

| **Situation Summary** Combined income: $215,000 · Robert: $125K, corporate VP · Sandra: $90K, school principal · Retirement: Robert $680K 401k / Sandra $420K 403b + pension · Mortgage: $87,000 remaining, 3.25%, 6 years left · Kids: 2, both out of household · Emergency fund: $45,000 · Target retirement: Robert 65 / Sandra 62 |
| --- |

**01  YOUR SITUATION**

Robert and Sandra, you've built something real. At 58 and 56, with $1.1M in retirement savings, a mortgage that's almost gone, two kids launched, and a combined income in the top 10% of American households — you're in the best financial position you've ever been in. The question isn't whether you've done enough. The question is whether the next 7 years are optimized to get you both across the finish line at the ages you want. And there are three decisions in that window that will matter more than anything else you do.

**02  FINANCIAL SNAPSHOT**

Combined take-home: approximately $12,800/month · Retirement savings: $1,100,000 (Robert $680K, Sandra $420K + pension) · Mortgage: $87,000 remaining · Emergency fund: $45,000 (3.5 months) · Sandra's pension: projects to approximately $[X]/month at age 62 · Combined estimated Social Security (at full retirement age): approximately $[Y]/month

**03  WHAT YOU****'****RE CARRYING**

1. Sequence of returns risk — with Robert's portfolio at $680K and retirement 7 years away, a market downturn in years 5-8 of retirement would be significantly more damaging than one earlier. The asset allocation question becomes critical in the next 3 years. 2. Social Security timing — Robert claiming at 65 vs. 70 represents an estimated $[X]K in lifetime benefits. This decision interacts with Sandra's pension timing and the household's tax situation. 3. Healthcare bridge — Sandra's retirement at 62 means 3 years without employer healthcare before Medicare. This gap needs to be budgeted and planned.

**04  YOUR OPPORTUNITIES**

Catch-up contributions: Both Robert and Sandra are over 50 and eligible for catch-up contributions. At your income and tax bracket, maximizing these contributions — 401k at $30,500/year each — reduces your tax bill significantly and accelerates portfolio growth in the highest-return years remaining. · Sandra's pension optimization: The pension decision — single life vs. joint and survivor annuity, and timing — deserves a detailed analysis before Sandra gives notice. The difference can be $[X]/month over a 30-year retirement.

**05  WHAT TO DO FIRST**

1. Maximize catch-up contributions immediately — if you're not already at $30,500 for both 401k contributions, adjust your payroll deductions this week. The tax benefit alone is significant at your bracket. 2. Schedule a Social Security analysis — the break-even analysis for Robert at 65 vs. 70 is calculable given your health history and household situation. Do this before making any retirement date decisions. 3. Get a pension optimization projection from Sandra's district — request the comparison of single-life vs. joint-and-survivor payouts before you're within 12 months of the decision.

**06  MOVING FORWARD**

The foundation is solid. What Robert and Sandra need now is sequencing — getting the next seven years right in the right order. The Social Security decision, the pension optimization, the asset allocation shift, and the healthcare bridge are all connected. Making them well, in the right order, is the work of the next 24 months. You have the resources. You need the clarity.

**USER SIMULATION 3**

**Danielle, 41**

| **41** Age | **Starting Over** Life Phase | *Recently divorced, returning to full-time work after 8 years, two school-age kids, rebuilding* |
| --- | --- | --- |

| **Situation Summary** Income: $67,000 (restarting career) · Kids: 9 and 11, 50/50 custody · Alimony: $2,200/month for 3 years · Child support received: $1,100/month · Retirement: $45,000 IRA (from divorce settlement) · Mortgage: Staying in marital home, refinanced in her name at $1,890/month · Emergency fund: $3,200 |
| --- |

**01  YOUR SITUATION**

Danielle, you're doing something harder than most people realize: rebuilding a financial life from scratch at 41, while raising two kids, restarting a career after eight years, and navigating a transition that changed everything at once. You're not starting from zero — you have a home, an IRA, and alimony that gives you a window to stabilize. But the window has a hard close date. Here is the honest picture of where you are and what needs to happen in the next 36 months.

**02  FINANCIAL SNAPSHOT**

Monthly income: $5,583 take-home + $2,200 alimony + $1,100 child support = $8,883/month total · IRA: $45,000 · Emergency fund: $3,200 (0.4 months of expenses) · Mortgage: $1,890/month · Alimony window: 3 years remaining · Net worth: approximately $[home equity] + $45,000 IRA minus any remaining debt

**03  WHAT YOU****'****RE CARRYING**

1. Alimony cliff — in 36 months, $2,200/month of income disappears. Your financial plan needs to be built to survive that cliff, not around it. 2. Emergency fund inadequacy — $3,200 covers less than two weeks of expenses for a single parent. One car repair or medical bill becomes a crisis. This is the highest-priority stabilization task. 3. Career income trajectory — returning to work at $67,000 after eight years out is a starting point, not a ceiling. The next 36 months of career investment directly impact your long-term financial security in a way they didn't when you were in a dual-income household.

**04  YOUR OPPORTUNITIES**

Head of Household filing status — as a single parent with primary or 50/50 custody, you qualify for Head of Household filing status, which is more favorable than Single. This reduces your tax bill by approximately $[X] per year. · Dependent care FSA — if your employer offers one, a Dependent Care FSA reduces childcare costs for after-school care and summer programs by approximately $[X] per year through pre-tax contributions. · IRA growth window — your $45,000 IRA has 24 years until retirement. At a 7% return, it grows to approximately $230,000 without adding another dollar. Contributing even $200/month accelerates that significantly.

**05  WHAT TO DO FIRST**

1. Build a $10,000 emergency fund — before anything else. This is the stabilization that makes every other decision less fragile. Target: 12 months to reach it through automatic transfer. 2. Verify Head of Household filing status with your tax preparer — if you're not already filing this way, amend last year's return if eligible. 3. Enroll in your employer's Dependent Care FSA during the next open enrollment window.

**06  MOVING FORWARD**

What's true about Danielle's situation: she is not behind. She is starting. That is different. The IRA is real. The home equity is real. The career is real. The next 36 months — before the alimony window closes — are the most important financial years of her life. Not because of the alimony. Because of what she builds in the time she has it. The goal is to reach month 37 with a career trajectory, an emergency fund, and a financial structure that doesn't require the alimony to work. That is achievable.

## **What These Simulations Demonstrate**

Three very different people. Three very different life phases. Three reports that could not have been generated for anyone else.

- Maya's report knows she selected an HDHP without opening an HSA. It doesn't suggest she "consider an HSA" — it tells her to open one today and walks her through why.

- Robert and Sandra's report knows the specific numbers: $680K vs. $420K split, Sandra's pension complicating the timeline, the 7-year window and what's at stake in it.

- Danielle's report knows the alimony cliff is coming. It doesn't suggest she "create a budget" — it names the countdown and builds the entire guidance around surviving it.

This is the recognition standard. Not comprehensiveness. Not generic financial advice. The felt sense that Krovos has read their situation and is speaking directly to them.
