## **About This Library**

The Recognition Library is a catalog of specific life situations that Krovos's guidance engine must be able to identify and respond to with genuine specificity. Each pattern defines: the situation, the signals that indicate it, the primary burden to surface, the hidden opportunity the person may not have considered, and the difference between a generic and a recognized response.

This library is not a demographic taxonomy. It is not a list of customer personas. It is a catalog of the actual human situations that people bring to Krovos — situations defined by what is happening in their lives, not by who they are.

| *Recognition is the brand**'**s highest expression. When a person reads something Krovos has generated about their situation and thinks **"**that**'**s exactly me**"** — that is the product at its best.* |

**PATTERN 1**

**Dual-Income Household With Young Children**

| **Signals** · Two W-2 incomes from different employers · Children under 5 in household · Mortgage balance present · Both partners have retirement accounts **Primary Burden** The household is carrying maximum complexity at a time of maximum financial pressure — two careers to manage, childcare costs, a mortgage, and retirement savings all competing for limited cash flow. | **Hidden Opportunity** With two incomes and two employer benefit packages, there are often optimization opportunities being missed: one partner's FSA combined with the other's HSA, overlapping life insurance coverage that can be rationalized, and employer match rates that neither partner has fully maximized. |

**PATTERN 2**

**First-Time Homebuyer in Active Process**

| **Signals** · Rent payments present in current spending · Pre-approval or mortgage shopping signals · Emergency fund at or near minimum · Savings trajectory suggests down payment accumulation **Primary Burden** The combination of impending financial commitment, unfamiliar process, and high stakes creates acute anxiety. Most people in this situation don't know what they don't know about the process — from rate locks to inspection contingencies to closing costs. | **Hidden Opportunity** Most first-time homebuyers underestimate total closing costs by 30-50% and don't know about first-time homebuyer programs, down payment assistance, or the difference between pre-qualification and pre-approval. There may also be a rate buy-down opportunity that changes the math significantly. |
| --- | --- |

| **Generic response ✗** *Make sure you have enough saved for your down payment and closing costs before proceeding.* | **Recognized response ✓** At your current mortgage range, closing costs will likely run $[X]–$[Y] — on top of the down payment. [State] has a first-time homebuyer program that could offset $[Z] of that. Here is what you need to qualify and how to apply before you go under contract. |

**PATTERN 3**

**Single Earner Supporting Household**

| **Signals** · One W-2 income · Partner present in household with no income detected · Mortgage or significant rent · Multiple dependents **Primary Burden** The weight of being the only income for a household with dependents creates a specific kind of financial anxiety — the awareness that any disruption to employment would be catastrophic. This person often hasn't confronted the disability insurance gap because doing so requires engaging with an uncomfortable scenario. | **Hidden Opportunity** Single earners supporting households almost universally underinsure for disability. The standard group disability policy (if it exists at all) covers 60% of base salary with a waiting period. For a household where one income covers everything, that gap can be devastating. |
| --- | --- |

| **Generic response ✗** *Consider reviewing your insurance coverage to ensure your family is protected.* | **Recognized response ✓** If you were unable to work for 90 days, your household would have approximately $[X] in liquid reserves. Your current group disability coverage — if it activates — would replace 60% of your base salary after a 90-day waiting period. Here is what the gap looks like in real numbers and what it costs to close it. |

Most people in this phase have also never considered the sequence of returns risk — the unique danger of a market downturn in the first years of retirement. |

**PATTERN 5**

**Recent Career Change or Job Loss**

| **Signals** · Previous employer income no longer current · Gap in income or reduced income detected · Emergency fund potentially depleting · Benefits coverage status uncertain **Primary Burden** Career transition creates cascading uncertainty: income uncertainty, benefits uncertainty, retirement account uncertainty, and often identity uncertainty. Most people leave their old 401k at their former employer indefinitely — paying higher fees and losing consolidated visibility. A career transition is also the moment to reassess the entire benefits picture from scratch. |

**PATTERN 10**

**Retirement Account Mismatch**

| **Signals** · High 401k or 457b balance at one employer · Roth IRA absent or underfunded despite income eligibility · Tax bracket in accumulation phase suggests Roth optimization potential · Years to retirement suggest compounding opportunity **Primary Burden** Many people default to traditional pre-tax retirement contributions without considering whether Roth contributions would be more advantageous at their current income level and tax situation. This decision, compounded over decades, has significant consequences. | **Hidden Opportunity** A household in the 22-24% tax bracket during accumulation who switches to Roth contributions locks in today's tax rate rather than deferring to an unknown future rate. If tax rates rise — as many projections suggest — this can represent a significant long-term advantage. |

## **About the Recognition Standard**

Every Life Understanding Report should be evaluated against fifteen recognition tests — one for each pattern type most likely to be active for a given user. The standard is not "did we mention this topic?" The standard is "did the user feel seen specifically?"

A report that says "consider reviewing your insurance coverage" has failed the recognition test. A report that says "your household has a specific gap between your current disability coverage and what you would need if [primary earner] couldn't work for six months — here is what that gap looks like in real numbers" has passed it.

The Recognition Library is a living document. New patterns should be added as Krovos encounters new life situations through real users. The goal is not comprehensiveness — it is a deep catalog of the most common and consequential situations, described with enough specificity to guide genuine recognition.
